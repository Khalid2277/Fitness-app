# Detailed Exercise Guides

## Barbell Bench Press

### Proper Form and Technique
Lie on a flat bench with your eyes directly under the bar. Plant your feet firmly on the floor, creating a slight arch in your upper back by driving your shoulder blades together and down into the bench. Grip the bar with hands slightly wider than shoulder width, typically with your ring finger or pinky on the knurling rings. Unrack the bar with straight arms and position it directly over your mid-chest. Lower the bar in a controlled manner to your lower chest or upper sternum, touching lightly. Press the bar back up and slightly toward the rack in a slight arc, locking out over your shoulders. Maintain the scapular retraction and foot contact throughout the entire set.

### Common Mistakes and How to Fix Them
Flaring the elbows to 90 degrees places excessive stress on the shoulder joint and reduces pec activation. Keep elbows at approximately 45-75 degrees relative to the torso. Bouncing the bar off the chest reduces time under tension and increases injury risk. Touch the chest with control and pause briefly before pressing. Lifting the hips off the bench indicates the weight is too heavy or the lifter is compensating for weak pressing muscles. Keep the glutes in contact with the bench at all times. Losing scapular retraction during the press reduces the stable base and shifts stress to the shoulders. Actively squeeze the shoulder blades together before unracking and maintain this throughout.

### Muscles Worked
Primary: Pectoralis major (sternal and clavicular heads, approximately 85% activation of MVC). Secondary: Anterior deltoid (approximately 60% MVC), triceps (approximately 55% MVC). Stabilizers: Core, forearms, rotator cuff.

### Variations and Progressions
Close-grip bench press (hands shoulder-width) emphasizes triceps. Wide-grip bench press emphasizes pecs but increases shoulder stress. Paused bench press (2-3 second pause on chest) builds strength off the chest and eliminates the stretch reflex. Floor press limits range of motion and builds lockout strength. Dumbbell bench press allows greater range of motion and independent arm movement.

### Programming Recommendations
Place the bench press first in your pushing session when you are freshest. For strength: 4-5 sets of 3-5 reps at 82-90% 1RM, rest 3-5 minutes. For hypertrophy: 3-4 sets of 6-10 reps at 70-80% 1RM, rest 2-3 minutes. Bench 1-2 times per week depending on split.

### Safety Considerations
Always use a spotter or safety pins/arms when training heavy. Learn the "roll of shame" as a self-rescue technique. Do not use a thumbless (suicide) grip, as the bar can slip forward off the palms. Warm up with 2-4 progressively heavier sets before working weight.

### Who Should and Shouldn't Do This Exercise
The bench press is suitable for all experience levels with appropriate load selection. Individuals with shoulder impingement may need to limit range of motion or substitute dumbbell pressing, which allows the shoulders to rotate naturally. Those with wrist pain should ensure a straight wrist alignment or use wrist wraps.

## Incline Dumbbell Press

### Proper Form and Technique
Set an adjustable bench to 30-45 degrees. Sit with dumbbells resting on your thighs, then kick them up to shoulder level as you lean back into the bench. Retract your shoulder blades and press the dumbbells upward, converging slightly at the top without clanking them together. Lower under control until the dumbbells reach the level of your outer chest, feeling a stretch across the upper pectorals. Press back up to full extension.

### Common Mistakes and How to Fix Them
Setting the incline too steep (above 45 degrees) shifts the emphasis to the anterior deltoids rather than the upper chest. Use 30 degrees for maximum upper pec activation. Not going deep enough reduces the stretch on the pecs. Lower the dumbbells until your upper arms are at least parallel to the floor. Flaring the elbows excessively increases shoulder impingement risk. Keep elbows at a 45-60 degree angle relative to the torso.

### Muscles Worked
Primary: Pectoralis major (clavicular head, approximately 80% MVC). Secondary: Anterior deltoid (approximately 65% MVC), triceps (approximately 50% MVC).

### Variations and Progressions
Neutral-grip incline dumbbell press (palms facing each other) reduces shoulder stress. Single-arm incline press adds a core stability component. Incline dumbbell fly in the stretch position is a variation that maximizes the lengthened position stimulus.

### Programming Recommendations
Place after the main compound press. For hypertrophy: 3-4 sets of 8-12 reps, rest 90-120 seconds. The dumbbell variation is inherently less stable than barbell, so use moderate loads and focus on controlled form.

## Barbell Back Squat

### Proper Form and Technique
Position the bar across your upper traps (high bar) or rear deltoids and mid-traps (low bar). Grip the bar firmly and squeeze your shoulder blades together to create a muscular shelf. Unrack the bar and take 2-3 steps back. Set your feet shoulder-width apart or slightly wider, with toes pointed out 15-30 degrees. Initiate the descent by simultaneously breaking at the hips and knees, sitting down between your legs. Descend until the hip crease drops below the top of the kneecap (parallel or below). Drive up through the full foot, maintaining an upright torso. Lock out the hips and knees at the top. Keep your core braced using the Valsalva maneuver (deep breath, brace abs, hold) throughout the entire repetition.

### Common Mistakes and How to Fix Them
Knees caving inward (valgus) during the ascent indicates weak hip abductors or poor motor control. Cue "push your knees out over your toes." Excessive forward lean shifts stress to the lower back. Address ankle mobility limitations, strengthen the quads, and consider front squats or heel-elevated squats. Rising on the toes indicates weight is shifting forward. Focus on driving through the midfoot and heels. Not reaching adequate depth limits quadriceps and glute activation. Improve ankle and hip mobility, and use a box squat to establish consistent depth.

### Muscles Worked
Primary: Quadriceps (approximately 80% MVC), gluteus maximus (approximately 70% MVC). Secondary: Hamstrings (approximately 40% MVC), adductors (approximately 50% MVC), erector spinae (isometric stabilization), core (isometric stabilization).

### Variations and Progressions
High bar squat (Olympic style): more upright torso, greater quad emphasis. Low bar squat (powerlifting style): more forward lean, greater posterior chain emphasis, allows heavier loads. Pause squat: 2-3 second pause in the hole to build strength out of the bottom. Tempo squat: 3-4 second eccentric to increase time under tension. Box squat: squat to a box to reinforce consistent depth and develop concentric strength.

### Programming Recommendations
Place squats first in any lower body session. For strength: 4-5 sets of 3-5 reps at 82-90% 1RM, rest 3-5 minutes. For hypertrophy: 3-4 sets of 6-10 reps at 70-80% 1RM, rest 2-3 minutes. Squat 2 times per week for optimal frequency.

### Safety Considerations
Always squat in a power rack with safety bars set at appropriate height. Learn to fail safely by sitting back onto the safety bars rather than dumping the bar forward. Use a belt for sets above 80% 1RM if desired, but develop core strength without a belt during moderate-intensity work.

## Romanian Deadlift

### Proper Form and Technique
Stand with feet hip-width apart, holding a barbell with an overhand grip at arm's length in front of your thighs. With a slight bend in the knees (15-20 degrees), hinge at the hips by pushing your glutes back while maintaining a flat back. Lower the bar along the front of your legs, keeping it as close to your body as possible. Descend until you feel a deep stretch in the hamstrings, typically when the bar reaches mid-shin or just below the knees. Drive your hips forward to return to the standing position, squeezing the glutes at the top. The knees should not bend further during the descent; all movement comes from the hip hinge.

### Common Mistakes and How to Fix Them
Rounding the lower back occurs when the lifter goes beyond their hamstring flexibility or uses too much weight. Stop the descent when you feel the lower back beginning to round. Bending the knees excessively turns the RDL into a conventional deadlift. Maintain the initial slight knee bend throughout. Letting the bar drift away from the body increases the moment arm on the lower back. Keep the bar in contact with or within an inch of the legs throughout. Looking up and hyperextending the neck. Maintain a neutral head position by looking at a spot on the floor 6-8 feet ahead.

### Muscles Worked
Primary: Hamstrings (approximately 80% MVC), gluteus maximus (approximately 75% MVC). Secondary: Erector spinae (isometric, approximately 70% MVC), forearms (grip). The RDL is one of the most effective exercises for loading the hamstrings in a lengthened position, which recent research suggests is superior for hypertrophy.

### Programming Recommendations
For hypertrophy: 3-4 sets of 8-12 reps, rest 2-3 minutes. For strength: 3-4 sets of 5-8 reps. Place after squats on a lower body day, or as the primary hip hinge movement on a pull day. The RDL should be a staple in any program for posterior chain development.

## Conventional Deadlift

### Proper Form and Technique
Stand with feet hip-width apart, toes under the bar so the bar is over the mid-foot. Hinge at the hips and grip the bar just outside your legs using a double overhand or mixed grip. Drop your hips until your shins nearly touch the bar, chest up, back flat, shoulders slightly ahead of the bar. Brace your core with the Valsalva maneuver. Drive through the floor by straightening the legs and hips simultaneously. Keep the bar in contact with the legs throughout. Lock out by squeezing the glutes and standing tall without hyperextending the lower back. Lower the bar by hinging at the hips first, then bending the knees once the bar passes them.

### Common Mistakes and How to Fix Them
Rounding the lower back is the most dangerous error. If you cannot maintain a flat back, reduce the weight or improve hip mobility. Hips shooting up first (stiff-leg pattern) shifts all stress to the lower back. Cue "push the floor away" and think about leg pressing the ground. Jerking the bar off the floor can cause bicep tears (especially with mixed grip) and back injuries. Take the slack out of the bar before pulling by applying gradual tension. The bar drifting forward away from the body increases lower back stress. Drag the bar up the shins and thighs.

### Muscles Worked
Primary: Posterior chain collectively — hamstrings, glutes, erector spinae (all approximately 70-85% MVC). Secondary: Quadriceps (off the floor), lats (keeping bar close), traps (lockout), forearms (grip). The deadlift recruits more total muscle mass than any other exercise.

### Programming Recommendations
For strength: 3-5 sets of 1-5 reps at 85-95% 1RM, rest 3-5 minutes. For hypertrophy: 3-4 sets of 5-8 reps at 70-80% 1RM. Deadlift once or twice per week. Due to the high systemic fatigue generated, conventional deadlifts are typically programmed with lower volume than squats or bench press.

## Pull-Up

### Proper Form and Technique
Grip the bar with an overhand (pronated) grip slightly wider than shoulder width. Hang with arms fully extended and shoulder blades depressed (pulled down away from the ears). Initiate the pull by retracting and depressing the scapulae, then drive your elbows down and back toward your hips. Pull until your chin clears the bar or your chest approaches it. Lower under control to a full dead hang, achieving complete elbow extension before the next rep.

### Common Mistakes and How to Fix Them
Kipping or swinging uses momentum rather than muscle force. Perform strict reps with a brief pause at the dead hang. Half reps (not reaching full extension at the bottom) reduce the stretch on the lats. Extend fully between every rep. Not initiating with the scapulae means the biceps do the majority of the work. Think about pulling your shoulder blades into your back pockets before bending the elbows.

### Muscles Worked
Primary: Latissimus dorsi (approximately 85% MVC), teres major. Secondary: Biceps (approximately 60% MVC), brachialis, brachioradialis, lower traps, rhomboids, rear deltoids, core (anti-extension).

### Variations and Progressions
Chin-up (supinated grip): greater biceps involvement, slightly easier for most people. Neutral-grip pull-up: joint-friendly, balanced lat and biceps activation. Weighted pull-up: add weight via a belt or dumbbell between legs for progressive overload. Band-assisted pull-up: loop a band over the bar and under your knees for assistance. Negative-only pull-up: jump to the top and lower slowly for 3-5 seconds (builds strength for beginners who cannot do full pull-ups).

### Programming Recommendations
For strength: 4-5 sets of 3-5 reps (add weight as needed), rest 2-3 minutes. For hypertrophy: 3-4 sets of 6-12 reps, rest 90-120 seconds. If you can do more than 12 reps, add weight. If you can do fewer than 5, use band assistance or negatives.

## Barbell Row

### Proper Form and Technique
Stand with feet hip-width apart, holding a barbell with an overhand grip slightly wider than shoulder width. Hinge at the hips until your torso is at approximately 45 degrees to the floor (or lower for more lat emphasis). Let the bar hang at arm's length with slight elbow extension. Pull the bar toward your lower chest or upper abdomen, driving the elbows back and squeezing the shoulder blades together at the top. Lower under control to the starting position.

### Common Mistakes and How to Fix Them
Using excessive body English (jerking the torso upright to heave the weight) reduces back activation and increases injury risk. Use a weight that allows a controlled pull and a 1-second squeeze at the top. Standing too upright shifts emphasis from the mid-back to the traps and rear delts. Maintain the hip hinge position throughout the set. Pulling to the wrong position: pulling to the hips emphasizes the lats, pulling to the lower chest emphasizes the mid-back and rhomboids. Choose based on target muscle.

### Muscles Worked
Primary: Latissimus dorsi, middle trapezius, rhomboids (all approximately 70-80% MVC). Secondary: Rear deltoids, biceps (approximately 55% MVC), erector spinae (isometric), forearms.

### Programming Recommendations
For hypertrophy: 3-4 sets of 8-12 reps, rest 2 minutes. For strength: 4 sets of 5-8 reps. Place as the first or second exercise on a pull day. Row at least as much volume as you bench press for shoulder health.

## Overhead Press (Barbell)

### Proper Form and Technique
Unrack the bar from the front rack position with hands just outside shoulder width. Stand with feet hip-width apart, glutes and core braced. Press the bar straight up, moving your head slightly back as the bar passes your face, then forward again once the bar clears. Lock out with the bar directly over the midfoot, head through the window of the arms. Lower under control to the starting position at the clavicle.

### Common Mistakes and How to Fix Them
Excessive back lean turns the overhead press into a standing incline press and overloads the lumbar spine. Squeeze the glutes and brace the core to prevent arching. Pressing the bar in front of the face (forward path) is mechanically inefficient. The bar should travel straight up in a vertical line. Grip too wide reduces mechanical advantage. Hands should be just outside shoulder width.

### Muscles Worked
Primary: Anterior deltoid (approximately 80% MVC), lateral deltoid (approximately 55% MVC). Secondary: Triceps (approximately 65% MVC), upper trapezius, serratus anterior, core (anti-extension).

### Programming Recommendations
For strength: 4-5 sets of 3-6 reps, rest 3 minutes. For hypertrophy: 3-4 sets of 8-12 reps, rest 2 minutes. Press 1-2 times per week. The overhead press progresses more slowly than other compound lifts; micro-loading (0.5-1 kg increments) is highly recommended.

## Lateral Raise

### Proper Form and Technique
Stand with feet hip-width apart, holding dumbbells at your sides with a slight bend in the elbows. Raise the dumbbells out to the sides until your arms are parallel to the floor or slightly above, leading with the elbows. At the top, your upper arms should be in line with your shoulders. Lower under control, stopping just before the dumbbells touch your sides to maintain tension. Keep a slight forward lean at the torso to align the lateral deltoid with gravity.

### Common Mistakes and How to Fix Them
Using too much weight and compensating with momentum (swinging, shrugging, leaning). Use a weight that allows 12-15 controlled reps. Shrugging the traps during the raise takes emphasis off the lateral delts. Think about pushing the dumbbells away from you rather than lifting them up. Raising too high (above shoulder level) can cause shoulder impingement in susceptible individuals. Parallel to the floor is sufficient.

### Muscles Worked
Primary: Lateral deltoid (approximately 80% MVC). Secondary: Anterior deltoid, supraspinatus (initial 0-30 degrees of abduction), upper trapezius (if shrugging occurs).

### Variations
Cable lateral raise: provides constant tension throughout the entire range of motion, unlike dumbbells which have minimal tension at the bottom. Lean-away lateral raise: lean away from a support while raising with one arm to increase the resistance in the stretched position. Machine lateral raise: provides a fixed path and is useful for taking stabilization out of the equation.

### Programming Recommendations
For hypertrophy: 3-5 sets of 12-20 reps, rest 60-90 seconds. Lateral raises respond well to higher reps and metabolic stress techniques (drop sets, myo-reps). Train 3-4 times per week for optimal lateral delt development with 12-20 total weekly sets.

## Face Pull

### Proper Form and Technique
Set a cable machine with a rope attachment at face height. Grip the rope with a neutral grip, step back, and create tension. Pull the rope toward your face, separating the ends of the rope as you pull. At the end position, your upper arms should be parallel to the floor and externally rotated, with the rope beside or behind your ears and elbows flared. Squeeze the rear delts and external rotators for a 1-2 second hold. Return slowly.

### Common Mistakes and How to Fix Them
Using too much weight and turning it into a rowing motion with body lean. Use light weight and focus on the squeeze. Not externally rotating at the end — this is what distinguishes a face pull from a high row and what activates the rotator cuff. Pulling to the chest instead of the face reduces rear delt and external rotator engagement.

### Muscles Worked
Primary: Posterior deltoid (approximately 75% MVC), infraspinatus and teres minor (external rotators). Secondary: Middle trapezius, rhomboids.

### Programming Recommendations
For shoulder health and hypertrophy: 3-5 sets of 15-25 reps, rest 60 seconds. Include in every upper body or pull session. Face pulls are as much a preventive exercise as a hypertrophy exercise and should be treated as non-negotiable for shoulder health.

## Barbell Curl

### Proper Form and Technique
Stand with feet hip-width apart, holding a barbell with a supinated (underhand) grip at shoulder width or slightly wider. Start with the bar at arm's length against your thighs. Curl the bar upward by flexing the elbows, keeping the upper arms stationary and elbows pinned at your sides. Squeeze the biceps at the top when the bar reaches shoulder level. Lower under control for a 2-3 second eccentric.

### Common Mistakes and How to Fix Them
Swinging the body or using hip momentum to heave the weight up. Use a weight that allows strict form or perform curls with your back against a wall. Letting the elbows drift forward during the curl shifts tension to the anterior deltoid. Keep elbows fixed at your sides throughout. Incomplete range of motion at the bottom — fully extend the elbows between reps to maximize the stretch.

### Muscles Worked
Primary: Biceps brachii (long and short head, approximately 85% MVC). Secondary: Brachialis, brachioradialis, forearm flexors.

### Programming Recommendations
For hypertrophy: 3-4 sets of 8-12 reps, rest 60-90 seconds. Place after compound pulling exercises. Alternate between barbell curls and other bicep variations across sessions to train the biceps from different angles and muscle lengths.

## Tricep Pushdown

### Proper Form and Technique
Attach a straight bar, V-bar, or rope to a high cable pulley. Stand facing the machine with feet shoulder-width apart, elbows pinned at your sides. Start with elbows bent at approximately 90 degrees. Push the attachment down by extending the elbows fully. Squeeze the triceps at full extension for a 1-second hold. Return under control to the starting position without letting the elbows flare forward.

### Common Mistakes and How to Fix Them
Leaning over the weight and using bodyweight to push down. Stand upright with a slight lean and use only elbow extension. Letting the elbows drift forward turns this into a pressing movement. Keep upper arms locked at your sides. Using partial range of motion — extend fully and achieve a complete contraction.

### Muscles Worked
Primary: Triceps (lateral head primarily, approximately 80% MVC, with medial head contributing throughout). Secondary: Anconeus.

### Programming Recommendations
For hypertrophy: 3-4 sets of 10-15 reps, rest 60-90 seconds. Rope pushdowns allow for wrist pronation at the bottom, which enhances lateral head activation. Use as an accessory exercise after compound pressing.

## Leg Press

### Proper Form and Technique
Sit in the leg press machine with your back flat against the pad. Place feet shoulder-width apart on the platform, positioned in the middle or slightly high. Unrack the sled by straightening the legs and releasing the safety handles. Lower the sled by bending the knees toward your chest until the knees reach approximately 90 degrees of flexion. Press through the full foot to extend the legs without locking the knees fully at the top.

### Common Mistakes and How to Fix Them
Rounding the lower back at the bottom by going too deep. Only descend as far as your flexibility allows while maintaining lower back contact with the pad. Locking the knees at the top places dangerous stress on the joint. Stop just short of full extension. Placing feet too low on the platform increases knee stress. A higher foot position shifts emphasis to the glutes and hamstrings.

### Muscles Worked
Primary: Quadriceps (approximately 80% MVC). Secondary: Gluteus maximus, hamstrings, adductors. Foot placement determines emphasis: low and narrow = more quad, high and wide = more glute and hamstring.

### Programming Recommendations
For hypertrophy: 3-4 sets of 8-15 reps, rest 2 minutes. The leg press allows heavy loading with minimal spinal compression, making it an excellent complement to squats or a substitute for individuals with back issues.

## Leg Curl (Seated and Lying)

### Proper Form and Technique
**Seated leg curl:** Sit in the machine with the back pad adjusted so you feel a stretch on the hamstrings at the starting position. Place the pad behind your lower legs just above the ankles. Curl your legs downward and back, squeezing the hamstrings at full contraction. Return slowly, emphasizing the 2-3 second eccentric.

**Lying leg curl:** Lie face down on the machine with the pad behind your ankles. Curl your heels toward your glutes, squeezing at the top. Lower under control. Avoid lifting your hips off the pad.

### Muscles Worked
Primary: Hamstrings — biceps femoris, semitendinosus, semimembranosus (approximately 85% MVC). Secondary: Gastrocnemius (assists knee flexion). The seated leg curl produces higher hamstring EMG than the lying version because the hip-flexed position pre-stretches the hamstrings.

### Programming Recommendations
For hypertrophy: 3-4 sets of 10-15 reps, rest 60-90 seconds. Include in every leg session alongside a hip hinge movement (RDL, good morning) for complete hamstring development.

## Hip Thrust (Barbell)

### Proper Form and Technique
Sit on the floor with your upper back against a bench, knees bent, feet flat on the floor at hip width. Roll a loaded barbell over your legs until it rests in the crease of your hips (use a barbell pad for comfort). Plant your feet firmly with shins vertical at the top position. Drive through your heels and squeeze your glutes to raise your hips until your body forms a straight line from shoulders to knees. Hold the top position for 1-2 seconds with a hard glute squeeze. Lower under control.

### Common Mistakes and How to Fix Them
Hyperextending the lower back at the top instead of achieving hip extension through the glutes. Cue a posterior pelvic tilt (tucking the tailbone) at the top. Feet too far forward shifts emphasis to the hamstrings. Position feet so shins are vertical at the top. Pushing through the toes instead of the heels reduces glute activation.

### Muscles Worked
Primary: Gluteus maximus (approximately 85% MVC — the highest glute activation of any exercise according to Contreras et al. 2015). Secondary: Hamstrings, quadriceps, core.

### Programming Recommendations
For hypertrophy: 3-4 sets of 8-15 reps, rest 2 minutes. Can be used as a primary or secondary glute exercise. Hip thrusts are the single most effective exercise for glute hypertrophy and should be a staple in any lower body program.

## Calf Raise (Standing)

### Proper Form and Technique
Stand on a calf raise machine or the edge of a step with the balls of your feet on the surface and heels hanging off. Start by lowering your heels below the platform to achieve a deep stretch on the calves. Drive up onto your toes as high as possible, squeezing the calves at the peak contraction for 1-2 seconds. Lower slowly over 2-3 seconds for a full stretch.

### Muscles Worked
Primary: Gastrocnemius (maximally activated when knees are straight). Secondary: Soleus.

### Programming Recommendations
For hypertrophy: 4-5 sets of 10-15 reps, rest 60-90 seconds. Train calves 3-4 times per week for optimal results due to their endurance-oriented fiber composition and high recovery capacity. Combine with seated calf raises to target the soleus.

## Plank

### Proper Form and Technique
Place forearms on the floor with elbows directly under shoulders. Extend legs behind you, resting on toes. Create a straight line from head to heels. Brace the core by squeezing the abs as if preparing for a punch. Squeeze the glutes to prevent the hips from sagging. Hold for the prescribed time while breathing steadily.

### Muscles Worked
Primary: Transverse abdominis, rectus abdominis (isometric contraction). Secondary: Obliques, erector spinae, shoulders, quadriceps.

### Programming Recommendations
For core endurance: 3-4 sets of 30-60 second holds. Once 60-second holds are easy, progress to weighted planks (plate on back), long-lever planks (elbows further forward), or RKC planks (maximum tension for shorter holds of 10-20 seconds).

## Cable Crunch

### Proper Form and Technique
Kneel in front of a high cable with a rope attachment. Hold the rope at the sides of your head or behind your neck. Keeping the hips stationary, flex the spine by crunching the ribcage toward the pelvis. Contract the abs hard at the bottom of the movement. Return to the starting position under control without extending the spine fully (maintain tension).

### Common Mistakes and How to Fix Them
Hinging at the hips instead of flexing the spine — this uses the hip flexors rather than the abs. Focus on curling the ribcage down, not bending at the waist. Using too much weight and relying on momentum. Use a weight that allows a controlled 2-second concentric and 2-second eccentric.

### Muscles Worked
Primary: Rectus abdominis (approximately 80% MVC). Secondary: Obliques.

### Programming Recommendations
For hypertrophy: 3-4 sets of 12-20 reps, rest 60 seconds. Cable crunches provide progressive overload for the abs, which is difficult to achieve with bodyweight-only exercises.

## Dumbbell Fly

### Proper Form and Technique
Lie on a flat bench holding dumbbells above your chest with arms slightly bent (15-20 degree elbow bend maintained throughout). Lower the dumbbells in a wide arc until you feel a deep stretch across the chest, with upper arms at approximately chest level. Reverse the motion, squeezing the pecs to bring the dumbbells back together over the chest.

### Muscles Worked
Primary: Pectoralis major (sternal head, emphasis on the stretch position). Secondary: Anterior deltoid.

### Programming Recommendations
For hypertrophy: 3-4 sets of 10-15 reps, rest 60-90 seconds. Use moderate weight with controlled form. The fly is a stretch-position exercise for the chest, which recent research suggests provides a hypertrophy advantage.

## Lat Pulldown

### Proper Form and Technique
Sit at the lat pulldown machine with thighs secured under the pads. Grip the bar with an overhand grip slightly wider than shoulder width. Lean back slightly (10-15 degrees). Pull the bar toward your upper chest by driving your elbows down and back. Squeeze the lats at the bottom, then extend the arms fully at the top, allowing the shoulder blades to protract and elevate for a complete stretch.

### Muscles Worked
Primary: Latissimus dorsi (approximately 80% MVC). Secondary: Biceps, teres major, rhomboids, lower trapezius.

### Programming Recommendations
For hypertrophy: 3-4 sets of 8-12 reps, rest 90 seconds. An excellent alternative to pull-ups for lifters who cannot perform adequate pull-up volume. Vary grip width and orientation (wide overhand, close neutral, supinated) across sessions.

## Seated Cable Row

### Proper Form and Technique
Sit at the cable row station with feet on the footplates and knees slightly bent. Grip the handle (V-bar, wide bar, or rope). Start with arms extended and a slight forward lean to stretch the lats. Pull the handle toward your lower abdomen, driving the elbows back and squeezing the shoulder blades together. Hold the contraction for 1 second. Return to the starting position with a controlled eccentric, allowing the shoulders to protract forward.

### Muscles Worked
Primary: Latissimus dorsi, middle trapezius, rhomboids. Secondary: Biceps, rear deltoids, erector spinae.

### Programming Recommendations
For hypertrophy: 3-4 sets of 10-15 reps, rest 90 seconds. The cable row provides constant tension throughout the range of motion, making it excellent for time under tension and muscle activation. Use a variety of handles across sessions.

## Front Squat

### Proper Form and Technique
Position the bar across the front of the shoulders in either the clean grip (elbows high, bar resting on the anterior deltoids with fingertips under the bar) or cross-arm grip. Unrack and step back. Squat by breaking at the hips and knees simultaneously, keeping the torso as upright as possible. Descend to full depth. Drive up while maintaining high elbows and an upright chest.

### Muscles Worked
Primary: Quadriceps (greater emphasis than back squat due to upright torso position). Secondary: Gluteus maximus, core (significant anti-flexion demand).

### Programming Recommendations
For strength: 3-4 sets of 4-6 reps. For hypertrophy: 3-4 sets of 6-10 reps. Front squats typically use 70-85% of back squat weight. Excellent for quad development and as a corrective exercise for lifters who lean excessively in back squats.

## Bulgarian Split Squat

### Proper Form and Technique
Stand approximately 2 feet in front of a bench. Place the top of one foot on the bench behind you. Hold dumbbells at your sides or a barbell on your back. Lower your back knee toward the floor by bending the front knee, keeping the front shin vertical or slightly forward. Descend until the back knee nearly touches the floor or the front thigh reaches parallel. Drive through the front foot to return to standing.

### Muscles Worked
Primary: Quadriceps (front leg), gluteus maximus (front leg). Secondary: Hamstrings, adductors, hip stabilizers, core (balance demand).

### Programming Recommendations
For hypertrophy: 3-4 sets of 8-12 reps per leg, rest 90 seconds. Excellent for addressing bilateral strength imbalances and developing single-leg stability. Can serve as a primary or secondary lower body exercise.

## Lunges (Walking and Stationary)

### Proper Form and Technique
Stand with feet together, holding dumbbells at your sides. Step forward with one leg, lowering your hips until both knees are bent at approximately 90 degrees. The front shin should be vertical, and the back knee should hover just above the floor. Push through the front foot to step forward into the next lunge (walking) or return to standing (stationary).

### Muscles Worked
Primary: Quadriceps, gluteus maximus. Secondary: Hamstrings, adductors, calves, core.

### Programming Recommendations
For hypertrophy: 3-4 sets of 10-12 reps per leg. Walking lunges cover more distance and have a greater cardiovascular component. Reverse lunges place less stress on the front knee and are recommended for individuals with knee sensitivity.

## Dumbbell Shoulder Press

### Proper Form and Technique
Sit on a bench with back support set at 90 degrees. Hold dumbbells at shoulder height with palms facing forward. Press the dumbbells upward until arms are fully extended, converging slightly at the top. Lower under control to the starting position with the dumbbells at ear level.

### Muscles Worked
Primary: Anterior deltoid, lateral deltoid. Secondary: Triceps, upper trapezius, serratus anterior.

### Programming Recommendations
For hypertrophy: 3-4 sets of 8-12 reps, rest 2 minutes. The dumbbell variation allows a greater range of motion and more natural shoulder movement than the barbell version, making it more joint-friendly for many lifters.

## Hammer Curl

### Proper Form and Technique
Stand with feet hip-width apart, holding dumbbells at your sides with a neutral grip (palms facing your body). Curl the dumbbells up by flexing the elbows while maintaining the neutral grip throughout. Squeeze at the top and lower under control.

### Muscles Worked
Primary: Brachialis, brachioradialis. Secondary: Biceps brachii (long head emphasis due to neutral grip).

### Programming Recommendations
For hypertrophy: 3-4 sets of 10-15 reps. Hammer curls are essential for developing arm thickness, as the brachialis pushes the biceps up from underneath when developed. Alternate with supinated curls across sessions.

## Skull Crushers (Lying Triceps Extension)

### Proper Form and Technique
Lie on a flat bench holding an EZ-bar or straight bar with a narrow overhand grip. Extend the arms so the bar is directly above the shoulders. Lower the bar toward the forehead or just behind the crown of the head by bending only at the elbows. Keep the upper arms stationary and angled slightly back (toward the head, not perpendicular to the floor) to maintain tension on the triceps throughout. Extend the elbows to return to the starting position.

### Muscles Worked
Primary: Triceps — all three heads, with particular emphasis on the long head when the arms are angled slightly back. Secondary: Anconeus.

### Programming Recommendations
For hypertrophy: 3-4 sets of 8-12 reps, rest 90 seconds. Skull crushers are one of the most effective triceps exercises due to the stretch-position loading of the long head. If elbow pain occurs, switch to an overhead cable extension or use dumbbells.

## Cable Crossover

### Proper Form and Technique
Set both pulleys to the highest position on a cable crossover machine. Stand in the center with one foot slightly forward for stability. Grip the handles and extend arms out to the sides with a slight bend in the elbows. Pull the handles together in a wide arc in front of the chest, crossing the hands slightly at the end for maximum pec contraction. Return under control to the starting position, feeling a stretch across the chest.

### Muscles Worked
Primary: Pectoralis major (sternal and clavicular heads). Secondary: Anterior deltoid.

### Programming Recommendations
For hypertrophy: 3-4 sets of 12-15 reps, rest 60-90 seconds. The cable crossover provides constant tension and allows the arms to cross the midline, which fully shortens the pec fibers. Excellent as a finishing exercise for chest.

## Incline Bench Press (Barbell)

### Proper Form and Technique
Set an adjustable bench to 30-45 degrees in a power rack or Smith machine. Grip the bar slightly wider than shoulder width. Unrack and lower the bar to the upper chest, just below the clavicle. Press the bar straight up to lockout. Maintain scapular retraction throughout.

### Muscles Worked
Primary: Pectoralis major (clavicular head). Secondary: Anterior deltoid, triceps.

### Programming Recommendations
For strength: 4 sets of 5-8 reps. For hypertrophy: 3-4 sets of 8-12 reps. Use 30 degrees for maximum upper pec emphasis. Loads will be approximately 70-80% of flat bench press capacity.

## Pendlay Row

### Proper Form and Technique
Stand with feet hip-width apart, barbell on the floor. Hinge at the hips until your torso is parallel to the floor. Grip the bar at slightly wider than shoulder width. From a dead stop on each rep, explosively pull the bar to your lower chest. Lower the bar back to the floor and reset before each rep. Each rep starts from a dead stop, which eliminates the stretch reflex and builds concentric pulling power.

### Muscles Worked
Primary: Latissimus dorsi, middle trapezius, rhomboids. Secondary: Rear deltoids, biceps, erector spinae.

### Programming Recommendations
For strength and power: 4-5 sets of 3-6 reps. The dead stop eliminates momentum and builds explosive pulling strength. More taxing on the lower back than other row variations due to the strict horizontal torso position.

## T-Bar Row

### Proper Form and Technique
Straddle the T-bar handle or use a landmine setup. Grip the handle with both hands. Hinge at the hips with a flat back, torso at approximately 45 degrees. Pull the weight toward your upper abdomen, squeezing the shoulder blades together. Lower under control.

### Muscles Worked
Primary: Latissimus dorsi, middle trapezius, rhomboids. Secondary: Biceps, rear deltoids, erector spinae.

### Programming Recommendations
For hypertrophy: 3-4 sets of 8-12 reps. The T-bar row allows heavier loading than dumbbell rows and provides a neutral grip option that is easier on the wrists and elbows.

## Chest Dip

### Proper Form and Technique
Grip parallel dip bars and lift yourself to the top position with arms locked. Lean your torso forward approximately 15-30 degrees. Lower your body by bending the elbows until your upper arms are at least parallel to the floor, feeling a stretch across the chest. Press back up to the starting position.

### Muscles Worked
Primary: Pectoralis major (lower/sternal emphasis), triceps. Secondary: Anterior deltoid.

### Programming Recommendations
For hypertrophy: 3-4 sets of 8-15 reps. Add weight via a dip belt once bodyweight becomes too easy. The forward lean distinguishes the chest dip from the triceps dip (upright torso). Individuals with shoulder issues should exercise caution, as the deep stretch position can aggravate existing problems.

## Preacher Curl

### Proper Form and Technique
Sit at a preacher curl bench with your upper arms resting fully on the pad. Grip an EZ-bar or dumbbell with a supinated grip. Start with arms nearly fully extended (slight bend to protect the elbow). Curl the weight up by flexing the elbows, squeezing the biceps at the top. Lower under control for a 2-3 second eccentric.

### Muscles Worked
Primary: Biceps brachii (short head emphasis due to shoulder flexion). Secondary: Brachialis.

### Programming Recommendations
For hypertrophy: 3-4 sets of 8-12 reps. The preacher curl eliminates momentum and isolates the biceps by fixing the upper arm against the pad. It loads the biceps in a relatively lengthened position, which may provide a hypertrophy advantage.

## Leg Extension

### Proper Form and Technique
Sit in the leg extension machine with your back flat against the pad. Position the ankle pad just above your ankles. Extend your legs by straightening the knees until fully extended. Squeeze the quads at the top for 1-2 seconds. Lower under control with a 2-3 second eccentric.

### Muscles Worked
Primary: Quadriceps — all four heads, with particular emphasis on the rectus femoris and vastus medialis (VMO) at full extension.

### Programming Recommendations
For hypertrophy: 3-4 sets of 10-15 reps, rest 60-90 seconds. Leg extensions are an excellent isolation exercise for the quads and are particularly useful for developing the VMO (teardrop muscle). They are also a good option for individuals who cannot squat due to back issues. Despite outdated concerns about knee safety, leg extensions performed through a full range of motion with controlled loads are safe for healthy knees.

## Good Morning

### Proper Form and Technique
Position a barbell on your upper traps as you would for a back squat. Stand with feet shoulder-width apart. With a slight bend in the knees, hinge at the hips by pushing your glutes back, lowering your torso toward the floor. Maintain a neutral spine throughout. Descend until your torso is approximately parallel to the floor or until you feel a strong hamstring stretch. Drive your hips forward to return to standing.

### Muscles Worked
Primary: Hamstrings, erector spinae, gluteus maximus. Secondary: Core (anti-flexion).

### Programming Recommendations
For strength and hypertrophy: 3-4 sets of 8-12 reps with moderate weight. The good morning is an excellent exercise for building posterior chain strength but requires strict form. Start with light weight and progress slowly. It should not be treated as a max effort exercise.

## Farmer's Walk

### Proper Form and Technique
Pick up heavy dumbbells or farmer's walk handles from the floor using a deadlift pattern. Stand tall with shoulders back and down, chest up, and core braced. Walk with controlled, deliberate steps for the prescribed distance or time. Maintain an upright posture throughout without letting the shoulders round forward or the torso lean to one side.

### Muscles Worked
Primary: Trapezius (isometric), forearms (grip, approximately 90% MVC). Secondary: Core (anti-lateral flexion), shoulders, erector spinae, legs.

### Programming Recommendations
For grip strength and conditioning: 3-4 sets of 30-60 seconds or 30-50 meters, rest 90-120 seconds. Use straps only if grip is the limiting factor and the goal is to overload the upper body and core. The farmer's walk is one of the most functional exercises available, building full-body stability and grip strength that transfers to all other lifts.

## Ab Wheel Rollout

### Proper Form and Technique
Kneel on the floor with the ab wheel in front of you. Grip the handles and brace your core. Slowly roll the wheel forward by extending your arms and hips, lowering your torso toward the floor. Only go as far as you can maintain a flat lower back and control. Pull back to the starting position by contracting the abs and pulling the wheel toward your knees.

### Muscles Worked
Primary: Rectus abdominis, transverse abdominis (anti-extension). Secondary: Latissimus dorsi, shoulders.

### Programming Recommendations
For core strength: 3-4 sets of 6-12 reps. Progress from kneeling to standing rollouts over time. If you cannot perform even kneeling rollouts with control, start with stability ball rollouts or plank variations.

## Russian Twist

### Proper Form and Technique
Sit on the floor with knees bent and feet flat (or elevated for added difficulty). Lean back slightly to engage the core. Hold a weight plate, dumbbell, or medicine ball at chest level. Rotate your torso to one side, bringing the weight beside your hip. Rotate to the other side. Each rotation to one side counts as one rep.

### Muscles Worked
Primary: Obliques (internal and external). Secondary: Rectus abdominis, hip flexors.

### Programming Recommendations
For hypertrophy: 3 sets of 15-20 reps per side, rest 60 seconds. Use moderate weight and focus on controlled rotation from the thoracic spine, not swinging the arms.

## Hanging Leg Raise

### Proper Form and Technique
Hang from a pull-up bar with arms fully extended. Brace the core and raise your legs together until they are parallel to the floor or higher. For maximum rectus abdominis activation, curl your pelvis up at the top (posterior pelvic tilt) rather than just lifting the legs with the hip flexors. Lower under control without swinging.

### Muscles Worked
Primary: Rectus abdominis (especially lower portion), hip flexors. Secondary: Obliques, forearms (grip).

### Programming Recommendations
For hypertrophy: 3-4 sets of 8-15 reps. Progress from knee raises to straight-leg raises to toes-to-bar. If grip is the limiting factor, use ab straps that loop around the upper arms.

## Cable Lateral Raise

### Proper Form and Technique
Stand sideways to a low cable pulley. Grip the handle with the hand furthest from the machine (cross-body). With a slight bend in the elbow, raise your arm out to the side until it is parallel to the floor. Lower under control. The cable provides constant tension throughout the entire range of motion, unlike dumbbells which have minimal resistance at the bottom.

### Muscles Worked
Primary: Lateral deltoid (constant tension throughout the full range, approximately 80-85% MVC).

### Programming Recommendations
For hypertrophy: 3-4 sets of 12-20 reps per arm, rest 60 seconds. Cable lateral raises are arguably superior to dumbbell lateral raises for lateral deltoid hypertrophy due to the constant tension profile. They are particularly effective with slow eccentrics and as part of myo-rep or drop set protocols.
