# Nutrition Fundamentals for Fitness

## Energy Balance and TDEE

Energy balance is the relationship between calories consumed (energy intake) and calories expended (energy output). When intake exceeds output, the body stores excess energy primarily as adipose tissue and, under the right conditions, as skeletal muscle. When output exceeds intake, the body mobilizes stored energy, resulting in fat loss and potentially muscle loss if protein intake and training stimulus are insufficient.

### Total Daily Energy Expenditure (TDEE)

TDEE is the total number of calories burned in a 24-hour period and consists of four components. Basal Metabolic Rate (BMR) accounts for 60-70% of TDEE and represents the energy required for basic physiological functions at complete rest: organ function, cellular repair, thermoregulation, and neural activity. The Thermic Effect of Food (TEF) accounts for approximately 10% of TDEE and represents the energy cost of digesting, absorbing, and metabolizing nutrients. Non-Exercise Activity Thermogenesis (NEAT) accounts for 15-30% of TDEE and includes all physical activity outside of structured exercise: walking, fidgeting, occupational activity, and household tasks. NEAT is the most variable component and can differ by 2000 kcal per day between sedentary and highly active individuals. Exercise Activity Thermogenesis (EAT) typically accounts for only 5-10% of TDEE for most people training 3-6 hours per week.

### TDEE Calculation Methods

**Mifflin-St Jeor Equation** (most accurate for the general population based on validation studies):
- Men: BMR = (10 x weight in kg) + (6.25 x height in cm) - (5 x age in years) - 5
- Women: BMR = (10 x weight in kg) + (6.25 x height in cm) - (5 x age in years) - 161

**Harris-Benedict Equation** (older, slightly less accurate, tends to overestimate):
- Men: BMR = 88.362 + (13.397 x weight in kg) + (4.799 x height in cm) - (5.677 x age in years)
- Women: BMR = 447.593 + (9.247 x weight in kg) + (3.098 x height in cm) - (4.330 x age in years)

**Katch-McArdle Equation** (most accurate when lean body mass is known):
- BMR = 370 + (21.6 x lean body mass in kg)

Once BMR is calculated, it is multiplied by an activity factor to estimate TDEE. Sedentary (desk job, minimal exercise): BMR x 1.2. Lightly active (light exercise 1-3 days/week): BMR x 1.375. Moderately active (moderate exercise 3-5 days/week): BMR x 1.55. Very active (hard exercise 6-7 days/week): BMR x 1.725. Extremely active (very hard exercise, physical job): BMR x 1.9.

These multipliers are estimates and should be used as starting points. The most reliable method for determining TDEE is to track body weight and caloric intake over 2-4 weeks and calculate the intake at which weight remains stable.

## Protein

Protein is the most critical macronutrient for individuals engaged in resistance training. It provides the amino acid building blocks required for muscle protein synthesis (MPS), supports immune function, enzyme production, and hormone synthesis, and has the highest thermic effect of any macronutrient.

### Optimal Protein Intake for Muscle Building

The landmark meta-analysis by Morton et al. (2018), published in the British Journal of Sports Medicine, analyzed 49 studies with 1863 participants and concluded that protein supplementation significantly augmented resistance training-induced gains in fat-free mass and muscle strength. The analysis identified a breakpoint at 1.62 g/kg/day beyond which additional protein did not produce statistically significant further gains in lean mass. However, the 95% confidence interval extended to 2.2 g/kg/day, suggesting that intakes up to this level may benefit some individuals, particularly those in a caloric deficit, older adults with anabolic resistance, or advanced lifters with high training volumes.

Practical recommendations: consume 1.6-2.2 g of protein per kilogram of body weight per day. During a caloric deficit, aim for the higher end (2.0-2.2 g/kg) to maximize muscle preservation. During a surplus, 1.6-1.8 g/kg is generally sufficient since the anabolic environment created by the caloric surplus reduces the need for excess protein.

### Protein Distribution

Research by Areta et al. (2013) and Mamerow et al. (2014) demonstrated that distributing protein intake evenly across 4-5 meals throughout the day, with approximately 0.4-0.55 g/kg per meal (typically 30-50 g per meal for most lifters), optimizes muscle protein synthesis better than consuming the same total protein in fewer, larger meals or in a skewed distribution (small breakfast, small lunch, large dinner). Each protein-containing meal triggers a rise in MPS that lasts approximately 3-5 hours, after which MPS returns to baseline regardless of continued amino acid availability (the "muscle-full" effect).

### Leucine Threshold

Leucine is the most potent amino acid for triggering MPS via activation of the mTOR signaling pathway. Each meal should contain approximately 2.5-3 g of leucine to maximally stimulate MPS. This amount is found in approximately 25-30 g of high-quality animal protein (whey, eggs, chicken, beef) or 35-45 g of most plant proteins. Older adults (over 60) may require a higher leucine threshold of 3-4 g per meal due to anabolic resistance.

### Protein Quality: BV and DIAAS

Biological Value (BV) measures the proportion of absorbed protein that is retained by the body. Whey protein has a BV of 104 (above 100 due to the reference standard), whole eggs score 100, beef scores 80, and soy scores 74. The Digestible Indispensable Amino Acid Score (DIAAS) is the newer, more precise measure recommended by the FAO since 2013. DIAAS evaluates individual amino acid digestibility at the ileal level. Whey protein isolate has a DIAAS above 1.0, eggs score 1.13, chicken breast scores 1.08, milk scores 1.14, soy protein isolate scores 0.90, pea protein scores 0.82, and rice protein scores 0.42. Combining complementary plant proteins (rice + pea, legumes + grains) can achieve DIAAS scores comparable to animal proteins.

### Complete vs. Incomplete Proteins

Complete proteins contain all nine essential amino acids (histidine, isoleucine, leucine, lysine, methionine, phenylalanine, threonine, tryptophan, valine) in sufficient quantities. Most animal sources are complete. Most individual plant sources are incomplete, being limited in one or more essential amino acids: legumes are low in methionine, grains are low in lysine. However, consuming a variety of plant protein sources throughout the day easily achieves a complete amino acid profile; they do not need to be combined within the same meal.

## Carbohydrates

Carbohydrates are the body's preferred fuel source for moderate-to-high intensity exercise. They are stored as glycogen in muscles (approximately 300-500 g) and the liver (approximately 80-100 g), providing readily available energy for resistance training and high-intensity cardiovascular work.

### Types of Carbohydrates

Simple carbohydrates (monosaccharides and disaccharides) are quickly digested and absorbed, causing rapid blood glucose elevation. Sources include fruit (fructose + glucose), table sugar (sucrose), honey, and candy. They are useful immediately before, during, and after training when rapid energy delivery is beneficial.

Complex carbohydrates (polysaccharides) have longer molecular chains that require more time to digest, providing a slower, more sustained release of glucose. Sources include oats, rice, potatoes, sweet potatoes, whole-grain bread, pasta, and legumes. These should constitute the majority of daily carbohydrate intake.

### Glycemic Index and Glycemic Load

The Glycemic Index (GI) ranks carbohydrates on a scale of 0-100 based on how quickly they raise blood glucose relative to pure glucose (GI = 100). Low GI foods (55 or below) include oats, sweet potatoes, legumes, and most fruits. Medium GI foods (56-69) include brown rice, whole wheat bread, and bananas. High GI foods (70+) include white rice, white bread, potatoes, and watermelon. However, the practical relevance of GI is limited because the glycemic response is modified by the presence of protein, fat, and fiber consumed in the same meal, and by the total quantity consumed (Glycemic Load).

### Fiber

Fiber is a non-digestible carbohydrate that supports digestive health, promotes satiety, feeds beneficial gut bacteria, and helps regulate blood glucose and cholesterol levels. The recommended daily intake is 25-30 g for women and 30-38 g for men. Most adults consume only 15-17 g per day. Good sources include vegetables (broccoli, Brussels sprouts, carrots), fruits (berries, apples, pears), legumes (lentils, chickpeas, black beans), whole grains (oats, barley), and seeds (chia, flax). Excessively high fiber intake (above 50 g) can cause gastrointestinal distress and may impair mineral absorption.

### Carbohydrate Timing Around Training

**Pre-workout (1-4 hours before):** Consuming 1-4 g/kg of carbohydrates in the hours before training ensures glycogen stores are topped off and blood glucose is available. Closer to the workout, opt for lower-fiber, easily digestible sources to minimize gastrointestinal discomfort. A meal 2-3 hours before training containing 40-80 g of carbohydrates with moderate protein is ideal for most lifters.

**Intra-workout:** For training sessions lasting under 60-75 minutes, intra-workout carbohydrates are unnecessary for most lifters if pre-workout nutrition was adequate. For sessions exceeding 90 minutes, consuming 30-60 g/hour of fast-digesting carbohydrates (sports drink, dextrose, highly branched cyclic dextrin) can maintain performance.

**Post-workout:** Glycogen resynthesis begins immediately after training. Consuming 0.8-1.2 g/kg of carbohydrates within 2 hours post-workout, combined with protein, optimizes glycogen replenishment and creates a favorable anabolic environment. This is most critical when training twice per day or when the next training session is within 8 hours. For lifters training once daily with adequate total daily carbohydrate intake, post-workout carbohydrate timing is less critical.

### Recommended Carbohydrate Intake

For resistance training with a hypertrophy focus: 3-5 g/kg/day. For concurrent resistance and endurance training: 5-7 g/kg/day. During a fat loss phase, carbohydrates are typically the macronutrient that is reduced, but should not drop below 2-3 g/kg/day for extended periods to preserve training performance and hormonal function. During a bulking phase, carbohydrates should be high (4-7 g/kg/day) to fuel performance and support the anabolic environment.

## Fats

Dietary fat is essential for hormone production (including testosterone and estrogen), absorption of fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K), cell membrane integrity, brain function, and insulation of vital organs. Fat provides 9 kcal per gram, making it the most energy-dense macronutrient.

### Essential Fatty Acids

The two essential fatty acids that must be obtained from the diet are alpha-linolenic acid (ALA, an omega-3) and linoleic acid (LA, an omega-6). The body can convert ALA into the longer-chain omega-3s EPA and DHA, but this conversion is inefficient (less than 5-10%), making direct consumption of EPA and DHA from fatty fish (salmon, mackerel, sardines) or supplementation important.

### Omega-3 to Omega-6 Ratio

The modern Western diet provides an omega-6 to omega-3 ratio of approximately 15-20:1, far exceeding the evolutionary ratio of approximately 1-4:1. Excessive omega-6 intake relative to omega-3 promotes a pro-inflammatory state. While the absolute intake of omega-3s is more important than the ratio per se, reducing omega-6 intake (from processed vegetable oils like soybean, corn, and sunflower oil) and increasing omega-3 intake (from fatty fish, walnuts, flaxseed) supports an anti-inflammatory profile that may benefit recovery and body composition.

### Minimum Fat Intake

Fat intake should not drop below 0.5-1.0 g/kg/day (or approximately 15-20% of total calories) for extended periods. Below this threshold, testosterone production can be impaired. Dorgan et al. (1996) found that switching from a high-fat (40% of calories) to a low-fat (18% of calories) diet reduced serum testosterone by approximately 12%. For lifters aiming to maximize hormonal health during a cutting phase, maintaining fat intake at 0.7-1.0 g/kg is advisable.

### Types of Dietary Fat

Monounsaturated fats (olive oil, avocados, almonds, macadamia nuts) are associated with improved cardiovascular health and insulin sensitivity. Polyunsaturated fats include omega-3s (fatty fish, walnuts, flax) and omega-6s (vegetable oils, seeds). Saturated fats (red meat, dairy, coconut oil, butter) should be kept to less than 10% of total calories according to most health guidelines, though their role in cardiovascular disease is debated. Trans fats (partially hydrogenated oils, some processed foods) should be avoided entirely as they unequivocally increase cardiovascular disease risk.

## Micronutrients for Athletes

While macronutrients provide the caloric energy for training and recovery, micronutrients serve as cofactors for enzymatic reactions, support immune function, and regulate hormonal processes that are critical for performance and body composition.

### Vitamin D3

Vitamin D3 (cholecalciferol) functions as a steroid hormone precursor and is essential for calcium absorption, bone health, immune function, and muscle function. Deficiency (serum 25(OH)D below 30 ng/mL) is associated with reduced muscle strength, increased risk of stress fractures, and impaired immune function. Approximately 40-50% of the global population is vitamin D insufficient. Athletes who train indoors or live at high latitudes are at particular risk. Supplementation with 1000-5000 IU/day is recommended to maintain serum levels of 40-60 ng/mL, which is the range associated with optimal health and performance outcomes.

### Magnesium

Magnesium is involved in over 300 enzymatic reactions, including energy production, protein synthesis, muscle and nerve function, and blood glucose regulation. Approximately 50-60% of adults do not meet the RDA of 400-420 mg/day for men and 310-320 mg/day for women. Deficiency symptoms include muscle cramps, fatigue, sleep disturbances, and impaired recovery. Good food sources include dark leafy greens, nuts (almonds, cashews), seeds (pumpkin seeds), whole grains, and dark chocolate. Supplementation with magnesium glycinate (200-400 mg before bed) is well-absorbed and may improve sleep quality.

### Zinc

Zinc is critical for testosterone production, immune function, protein synthesis, and wound healing. The RDA is 11 mg/day for men and 8 mg/day for women. Zinc is found in red meat, shellfish (especially oysters, which contain 74 mg per serving), poultry, legumes, nuts, and seeds. Vegetarians and vegans are at higher risk of deficiency because plant-based zinc sources have lower bioavailability due to phytate content. Supplementation with 15-30 mg/day of zinc picolinate or zinc citrate is appropriate for those with confirmed deficiency.

### Iron

Iron is essential for oxygen transport (hemoglobin), energy production, and immune function. Deficiency is the most common nutritional deficiency worldwide and is particularly prevalent in female athletes, endurance athletes, and those on restrictive diets. Heme iron (from red meat, poultry, fish) has 15-35% absorption, while non-heme iron (from plants, fortified foods) has only 2-20% absorption. Vitamin C consumed alongside non-heme iron sources significantly improves absorption. The RDA is 8 mg/day for men and 18 mg/day for premenopausal women. Iron should only be supplemented based on blood work (serum ferritin below 30 ng/mL) as excess iron is toxic.

### Vitamin B12

Vitamin B12 is essential for red blood cell formation, neurological function, and DNA synthesis. It is found almost exclusively in animal products (meat, fish, dairy, eggs). Vegans must supplement with 250-500 mcg/day of cyanocobalamin or methylcobalamin. Deficiency causes megaloblastic anemia, fatigue, neurological symptoms (tingling, numbness), and cognitive impairment.

### Calcium

Calcium is the most abundant mineral in the body, with 99% stored in bones and teeth. It is essential for bone density, muscle contraction, nerve transmission, and blood clotting. The RDA is 1000 mg/day for adults under 50 and 1200 mg/day for women over 50. Food sources include dairy products, fortified plant milks, sardines with bones, tofu (calcium-set), and leafy greens (kale, bok choy). Calcium absorption is enhanced by vitamin D and inhibited by oxalates (spinach, rhubarb) and phytates.

## Hydration

Water is involved in virtually every metabolic process, including nutrient transport, temperature regulation, joint lubrication, and waste removal. Even mild dehydration of 2% body weight loss through sweat can reduce aerobic performance by up to 10% and impair cognitive function, mood, and concentration.

### Water Requirements

General recommendations for physically active individuals are 35-45 mL per kilogram of body weight per day. For an 80 kg lifter, this translates to 2.8-3.6 liters per day, not including additional fluid needed to replace sweat losses during training. During exercise, aim to consume 400-800 mL per hour depending on sweat rate, temperature, and humidity. Post-exercise, consume 1.5 liters of fluid for every kilogram of body weight lost during the session.

### Electrolytes

Sodium, potassium, magnesium, and calcium are the primary electrolytes lost through sweat. Sodium is lost in the greatest quantity (500-1500 mg per liter of sweat) and is the most critical to replace during prolonged or heavy exercise. For training sessions under 60 minutes, water alone is sufficient. For sessions exceeding 60 minutes or in hot/humid conditions, an electrolyte drink providing 300-700 mg sodium, 75-200 mg potassium, and 50-100 mg magnesium per liter is appropriate.

## Meal Timing

The question of whether meal timing significantly impacts body composition has been extensively studied. The consensus from meta-analyses is that total daily macronutrient intake is far more important than the specific timing of meals.

### The Anabolic Window Myth

The concept of a narrow "anabolic window" of 30-60 minutes post-workout during which protein must be consumed to maximize muscle growth has been largely debunked. Schoenfeld, Aragon, and Krieger (2013) conducted a meta-analysis and found that the evidence for a narrow post-workout window was weak when total daily protein intake was adequate. The post-exercise elevation in muscle protein synthesis persists for 24-48 hours in trained individuals, meaning that the urgency of immediate post-workout nutrition has been overstated. However, consuming protein within 2-3 hours post-workout is still a reasonable practice, especially if the pre-workout meal was consumed more than 3-4 hours prior.

### Practical Meal Timing Recommendations

Eat 3-5 protein-rich meals distributed every 3-5 hours to maximize the MPS response. Consume a mixed meal containing protein and carbohydrates 2-3 hours before training. If training in a fasted state, prioritize post-workout protein intake. Consume a protein-rich meal or shake within 2 hours of training. Casein protein or a protein-rich snack before bed (30-40 g) may slightly enhance overnight MPS, based on research by Res et al. (2012) and Trommelen et al. (2016).

## Pre-Workout Nutrition

The primary goals of pre-workout nutrition are to ensure adequate glycogen stores, provide circulating amino acids for MPS, minimize gastrointestinal discomfort, and maintain blood glucose stability during the session.

### Timing and Composition

**3-4 hours before training:** A full mixed meal containing 0.4-0.5 g/kg protein, 1-2 g/kg carbohydrates, and moderate fat. Examples: chicken breast with rice and vegetables, oatmeal with eggs and fruit, pasta with lean meat sauce.

**1-2 hours before training:** A smaller, easily digestible meal with lower fat and fiber. Examples: Greek yogurt with a banana, rice cakes with peanut butter and honey, a protein shake with a piece of fruit.

**30-60 minutes before training:** A light snack with fast-digesting carbohydrates. Examples: a banana, a handful of dried fruit, a sports drink, rice cakes with jam.

If training early in the morning without time for a full meal, a quick source of carbohydrates (30-50 g) plus 20-30 g of easily digestible protein (whey shake) consumed 30-45 minutes before is sufficient.

## Post-Workout Nutrition

Post-workout nutrition supports recovery by initiating glycogen resynthesis, stimulating muscle protein synthesis, and reducing muscle protein breakdown.

### Optimal Post-Workout Meal

Consume 0.4-0.5 g/kg of protein (25-50 g for most individuals) combined with 0.8-1.2 g/kg of carbohydrates within 2 hours of training. The combination of protein and carbohydrates is synergistic: insulin released in response to carbohydrates suppresses muscle protein breakdown, while amino acids from protein stimulate MPS. Fast-digesting protein sources (whey protein, egg whites) stimulate MPS more rapidly than slow-digesting sources (casein, meat), though the difference over a 24-hour period is minimal. Practical post-workout meals include a whey protein shake with a banana and oats, chicken breast with white rice, or a turkey sandwich on white bread with fruit.

### Glycogen Resynthesis

Muscle glycogen is depleted during resistance training by approximately 25-40%, depending on the volume and intensity of the session. Consuming 1.0-1.5 g/kg of carbohydrates within the first 2 hours post-training maximizes glycogen resynthesis rate. If the next training session is more than 24 hours away, the urgency of immediate glycogen replenishment is reduced, and normal dietary carbohydrate intake will restore glycogen before the next session.

## Thermic Effect of Food

The thermic effect of food (TEF), also called diet-induced thermogenesis, is the energy required to digest, absorb, transport, and metabolize nutrients. TEF accounts for approximately 10% of total daily energy expenditure but varies significantly by macronutrient.

Protein has the highest TEF at 20-30% of calories consumed. This means that for every 100 kcal of protein eaten, 20-30 kcal are expended in processing it. This is one reason high-protein diets are advantageous for fat loss: they effectively provide fewer net calories than an isocaloric diet lower in protein. Carbohydrates have a TEF of 5-10% of calories consumed. Complex carbohydrates with more fiber tend toward the higher end. Fats have the lowest TEF at 0-3% of calories consumed, meaning they are processed most efficiently and contribute the most net energy. Alcohol has a TEF of approximately 10-15%.

From a practical fat loss perspective, replacing 500 kcal of dietary fat with 500 kcal of protein could increase daily energy expenditure by 85-135 kcal through TEF alone. Over weeks and months, this difference is meaningful.

## Alcohol and Fitness

Alcohol (ethanol) provides 7 kcal per gram but has no nutritional value. Its effects on body composition and performance are overwhelmingly negative when consumed in excess, though moderate consumption can be managed within a fitness-focused lifestyle.

### Effects on Muscle Protein Synthesis

Parr et al. (2014) found that consuming alcohol (approximately 1.5 g/kg, equivalent to roughly 12 standard drinks for an 80 kg individual) after resistance training reduced muscle protein synthesis by 24% when consumed alone and by 37% when consumed with carbohydrates instead of protein. Even moderate alcohol consumption (2-3 drinks) following training may blunt the MPS response by 15-20%.

### Caloric Content

A standard drink contains approximately 14 g of alcohol (98 kcal from alcohol alone), but total caloric content varies by beverage. A 350 mL beer contains 150-200 kcal. A 150 mL glass of wine contains 120-150 kcal. A 45 mL shot of spirits contains 95-110 kcal. Mixed drinks with sugary mixers can contain 250-500 kcal per serving.

### Practical Guidelines

Limit alcohol to 1-2 drinks per occasion and no more than 3-4 drinks per week during a focused training and nutrition phase. Avoid alcohol within 3-4 hours of training. If consuming alcohol, prioritize protein intake alongside or before drinking. Choose lower-calorie options (spirits with zero-calorie mixers, dry wines, light beers) and account for alcohol calories within your daily budget. During a strict cutting phase, alcohol should be minimized or eliminated entirely.

### Effects on Sleep and Recovery

Even moderate alcohol consumption disrupts sleep architecture, reducing REM sleep and deep sleep stages that are critical for growth hormone release and cognitive recovery. Two drinks consumed within 4 hours of bedtime reduce sleep quality by approximately 24% in terms of restorative sleep phases, as measured by Ebrahim et al. (2013). Since sleep is one of the most powerful recovery tools, the indirect effect of alcohol on recovery through impaired sleep may be more significant than its direct effects on protein synthesis.

### Effects on Hormones

Chronic heavy alcohol consumption reduces testosterone levels and increases cortisol and estrogen. However, moderate consumption (1-2 drinks) has minimal acute hormonal effects in healthy individuals. The key distinction is between occasional social drinking and habitual heavy consumption; the former is manageable within a fitness lifestyle, while the latter is incompatible with optimal performance and body composition goals.

## Nutrient Density vs. Calorie Density

Understanding the distinction between nutrient density and calorie density is essential for constructing a diet that supports both performance and health. Nutrient-dense foods provide a high quantity of vitamins, minerals, fiber, and phytochemicals relative to their caloric content. Examples include leafy green vegetables (kale, spinach, broccoli), berries, lean meats, fish, eggs, legumes, and whole grains. Calorie-dense foods provide a high number of calories per gram or serving, which can be either nutrient-dense (nuts, avocados, olive oil, salmon) or nutrient-poor (candy, soda, fried foods, pastries).

During a fat loss phase, prioritizing nutrient-dense, low-calorie-density foods maximizes satiety per calorie consumed, making it easier to maintain a deficit. Foods high in water and fiber content (fruits, vegetables, soups, legumes) have low calorie density and fill the stomach, triggering satiety signals. During a bulking phase when caloric targets are high, incorporating calorie-dense foods (nut butters, oils, dried fruit, granola, whole milk) helps meet caloric goals without excessive food volume.

The volumetric eating approach, popularized by Barbara Rolls, suggests that humans tend to eat a consistent weight of food regardless of calorie content. By filling the plate with high-volume, low-calorie foods and reducing the proportion of calorie-dense foods, total caloric intake decreases naturally without conscious restriction or hunger. This strategy is particularly effective for overweight individuals beginning a fat loss phase.

## Practical Food Choices by Macronutrient

### High-Quality Protein Sources

The following foods provide approximately 25-30 g of protein per serving: chicken breast (150 g cooked, 165 kcal), turkey breast (150 g cooked, 160 kcal), lean beef (130 g cooked, 250 kcal), salmon (150 g cooked, 300 kcal), tuna (1 can drained, 140 kcal), egg whites (250 g or roughly 8 whites, 130 kcal), whole eggs (4 large, 280 kcal), Greek yogurt (250 g nonfat, 170 kcal), cottage cheese (250 g low-fat, 200 kcal), whey protein isolate (1 scoop 30 g, 120 kcal), tofu (300 g firm, 240 kcal), tempeh (130 g, 250 kcal), lentils (200 g cooked, 230 kcal, note: also contains 40 g carbohydrates).

Olive oil (1 tablespoon, 120 kcal, 14 g fat), avocado (1 medium, 240 kcal, 22 g fat), almonds (30 g or about 23 almonds, 170 kcal, 15 g fat), natural peanut butter (2 tablespoons, 190 kcal, 16 g fat), walnuts (30 g, 185 kcal, 18 g fat), fatty fish like salmon (150 g, 300 kcal, 18 g fat including 3-4 g omega-3), chia seeds (2 tablespoons, 140 kcal, 9 g fat), flaxseed (2 tablespoons ground, 75 kcal, 6 g fat), dark chocolate 85%+ (30 g, 170 kcal, 14 g fat).

## Calculating and Tracking Macros

### Step-by-Step Macro Calculation

Step 1: Calculate TDEE using the Mifflin-St Jeor equation multiplied by an activity factor. Step 2: Set the caloric target based on goal (subtract 300-500 kcal for fat loss, add 200-500 kcal for muscle gain, or eat at TDEE for maintenance). Step 3: Set protein at 1.6-2.2 g/kg of body weight. Multiply grams by 4 to get protein calories. Step 4: Set fat at 0.7-1.2 g/kg. Multiply grams by 9 to get fat calories. Step 5: Subtract protein and fat calories from total calories. Divide the remainder by 4 to get carbohydrate grams.

Example for an 80 kg male with TDEE of 2800 kcal aiming for fat loss at 2300 kcal: Protein: 2.0 g/kg x 80 = 160 g (640 kcal). Fat: 0.8 g/kg x 80 = 64 g (576 kcal). Remaining calories: 2300 - 640 - 576 = 1084 kcal. Carbohydrates: 1084 / 4 = 271 g. Daily targets: 160 g protein, 271 g carbohydrates, 64 g fat, totaling 2300 kcal.

### Using a Food Scale

Tracking accuracy depends heavily on measuring food portions. Eyeballing portions is notoriously inaccurate, with studies showing that most people underestimate calorie intake by 30-50% when not using a food scale. A digital kitchen scale that measures in grams is the most important tool for anyone serious about tracking macros. Weigh all solid foods raw or in the state specified by the database entry. Measure all liquids with measuring cups or by weight. Log everything consumed, including cooking oils, sauces, condiments, and beverages, as these hidden calories can account for 200-500 kcal per day.

### Dealing with Tracking Fatigue

Precise macro tracking is mentally demanding and is best used during dedicated cutting or lean bulking phases lasting 8-20 weeks. During maintenance phases or diet breaks, transitioning to simplified tracking (tracking protein only, or using hand-portion estimates) can prevent burnout while maintaining reasonable dietary control. A fist-sized portion of carbohydrates is approximately 30-40 g carbs, a palm-sized portion of protein is approximately 25-30 g protein, a thumb-sized portion of fat is approximately 10-15 g fat, and two cupped hands of vegetables is approximately one serving.
