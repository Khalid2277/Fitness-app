# Evidence-Based Supplement Guide

## Tier 1: Strong Evidence

### Creatine Monohydrate

Creatine monohydrate is the most extensively researched and consistently effective supplement for improving strength, power output, and muscle mass. It is endorsed by the International Society of Sports Nutrition (ISSN), the American College of Sports Medicine, and virtually every major sports nutrition authority.

**Mechanism of action:** Creatine is stored in skeletal muscle as phosphocreatine (PCr), which donates its phosphate group to regenerate ATP during short-duration, high-intensity contractions. By increasing intramuscular PCr stores by 20-40%, creatine supplementation extends the capacity to perform maximal-intensity work for an additional 1-2 seconds, allowing more reps at a given weight or slightly heavier loads. Over time, this increased training capacity leads to greater adaptations in muscle size and strength.

**Dosing protocol:** The standard dose is 3-5 grams per day, taken consistently. A loading phase of 20 g/day (split into 4 doses of 5 g) for 5-7 days accelerates muscle saturation but is not required; 3-5 g/day achieves full saturation within 3-4 weeks. Timing is not critical; creatine can be taken at any point during the day. Taking it with a carbohydrate-containing meal may slightly enhance uptake via insulin-mediated creatine transport, but the practical difference is minimal.

**Expected benefits:** Strength increases of 5-10% on compound lifts, lean mass gains of 1-2 kg over 4-12 weeks (partly due to intracellular water retention, which is beneficial for muscle physiology), improved high-intensity exercise capacity, faster recovery between sets, and enhanced glycogen resynthesis. Recent research also suggests cognitive benefits, particularly under conditions of sleep deprivation or mental fatigue (Avgerinos et al. 2018 meta-analysis found a significant positive effect on short-term memory and reasoning).

**Safety:** Creatine monohydrate has been studied in over 1000 clinical trials and is consistently shown to be safe for healthy adults at recommended doses. It does not cause kidney damage in individuals with healthy kidney function (Kreider et al. 2017 ISSN position stand). It does not cause dehydration or cramping; early anecdotal reports of these effects have been refuted by controlled research. The only consistent side effect is weight gain of 1-3 kg due to intracellular water retention, which is a feature of its mechanism rather than a negative effect.

**Form:** Creatine monohydrate is the gold standard. Alternative forms (creatine HCl, creatine ethyl ester, buffered creatine) have no demonstrated advantage over monohydrate and typically cost significantly more. Micronized creatine monohydrate dissolves more easily in water but has the same efficacy.

### Caffeine

Caffeine is the most widely consumed psychoactive substance in the world and has robust evidence supporting its ergogenic effects on exercise performance, including resistance training, endurance exercise, and high-intensity interval training.

**Mechanism of action:** Caffeine acts primarily as an adenosine receptor antagonist, blocking the binding of adenosine (a neurotransmitter that promotes sleepiness and reduces arousal). This increases neural excitability, alertness, pain tolerance, and perceived energy. Caffeine also stimulates catecholamine release (adrenaline and noradrenaline), enhances calcium release from the sarcoplasmic reticulum (improving muscle contractility), and increases fatty acid mobilization.

**Dosing protocol:** The effective dose for exercise performance is 3-6 mg per kilogram of body weight, consumed 30-60 minutes before training. For an 80 kg individual, this translates to 240-480 mg. Caffeine has a half-life of approximately 5-6 hours, meaning that half the dose is still active 5-6 hours after consumption. To avoid sleep disruption, caffeine should be consumed at least 8-10 hours before bedtime. Common sources include coffee (approximately 80-100 mg per 8 oz cup), pre-workout supplements (150-300 mg per serving), and caffeine pills (100-200 mg per tablet).

**Expected benefits:** Increases in maximal strength (1RM) of 2-5%, increased total training volume (more reps at a given weight), reduced perceived exertion (the same work feels easier), enhanced focus and concentration, increased pain tolerance, and improved endurance performance by 2-5%. The ISSN position stand on caffeine (Guest et al. 2021) confirms these benefits across multiple exercise modalities.

**Tolerance considerations:** Regular caffeine consumption leads to tolerance, reducing the perceived effects over time. Cycling caffeine intake (using it only on demanding training days, or taking 1-2 week breaks periodically) can maintain its ergogenic effects. Genetic variation in caffeine metabolism (CYP1A2 gene) means some individuals are fast metabolizers (experiencing stronger, shorter effects) while others are slow metabolizers (experiencing prolonged effects and greater sleep sensitivity).

**Side effects and cautions:** Doses above 6 mg/kg can cause anxiety, jitteriness, gastrointestinal distress, rapid heart rate, and insomnia. Individuals with anxiety disorders, cardiovascular conditions, or caffeine sensitivity should use lower doses or avoid caffeine. Caffeine is a mild diuretic, but at moderate doses this effect is offset by fluid consumed with caffeine-containing beverages and does not contribute to dehydration during exercise.

### Protein Powder

Protein powder is a convenient and cost-effective way to meet daily protein requirements. It is not inherently superior to whole food protein sources; its value lies in convenience, rapid absorption (for whey), and precision in macro tracking.

**Whey protein** is derived from milk during cheese production and is the most popular and well-researched protein supplement. Whey concentrate contains 70-80% protein with some lactose and fat. Whey isolate contains 90-95% protein with minimal lactose (suitable for most lactose-intolerant individuals). Whey hydrolysate is pre-digested for faster absorption but offers no meaningful advantage for most lifters. Whey has a high leucine content (approximately 10-12% by weight), making it highly effective for stimulating MPS. It is rapidly digested and absorbed, reaching peak blood amino acid levels within 60-90 minutes.

**Casein protein** is the other major milk protein, comprising approximately 80% of milk's total protein content. It forms a gel in the stomach, resulting in slow and sustained amino acid release over 5-7 hours. Casein is commonly recommended before bed to provide a steady supply of amino acids during the overnight fast. Research by Res et al. (2012) found that 40 g of casein consumed before sleep increased overnight muscle protein synthesis by 22% compared to placebo. Casein has a DIAAS of 1.0+ and is a complete protein with high leucine content.

**Plant-based protein** powders include pea protein (DIAAS 0.82), rice protein (DIAAS 0.42), soy protein isolate (DIAAS 0.90), hemp protein, and blends. Rice and pea protein blends are particularly effective because they complement each other's amino acid profiles (pea is high in lysine but low in methionine, while rice is the opposite). A dose of 35-45 g of plant protein blend provides a similar MPS response to 25-30 g of whey. Soy protein isolate is the highest quality single plant protein and has been shown to support muscle growth comparably to whey in multiple studies when doses are adequate.

### Vitamin D3

Vitamin D3 (cholecalciferol) is technically a hormone precursor that the body synthesizes from sunlight exposure on the skin. Deficiency and insufficiency are endemic worldwide, affecting 40-50% of the population and up to 70-80% of individuals in northern latitudes during winter months.

**Dosing protocol:** Supplementation with 1000-5000 IU per day of vitamin D3, taken with a fat-containing meal to enhance absorption (vitamin D is fat-soluble). The appropriate dose depends on baseline serum 25(OH)D levels, which should be measured via blood test. Target serum levels of 40-60 ng/mL (100-150 nmol/L) are associated with optimal health, immune function, bone density, and muscle function. Individuals with levels below 20 ng/mL may require higher loading doses (10,000 IU/day for 8-12 weeks) before transitioning to a maintenance dose.

**Benefits for athletes:** Adequate vitamin D status is associated with greater muscle strength, reduced injury risk, improved immune function (fewer upper respiratory infections), better bone density, and potentially improved testosterone levels in men who were previously deficient. Close et al. (2013) found that athletes with serum vitamin D above 40 ng/mL had significantly better muscle function than those below 20 ng/mL.

## Tier 2: Moderate Evidence

### Beta-Alanine

Beta-alanine is a non-essential amino acid that increases intramuscular carnosine concentrations. Carnosine acts as an intracellular pH buffer, reducing the accumulation of hydrogen ions during high-intensity exercise and delaying the onset of muscular fatigue.

**Dosing protocol:** 3.2-6.4 g per day, split into multiple doses of 0.8-1.6 g to minimize the paresthesia (tingling) side effect that occurs at higher single doses. Beta-alanine takes 4-8 weeks of consistent supplementation to fully saturate muscle carnosine stores. Timing is not important; it works through chronic loading, not acute effects.

**Expected benefits:** The ISSN position stand on beta-alanine (Trexler et al. 2015) concludes that it improves exercise capacity during high-intensity exercise lasting 1-4 minutes, such as sets of 15-30 reps, interval training, and endurance events. The effect size is modest but consistent (approximately 2.85% improvement in exercise capacity). It provides minimal benefit for short-duration, maximal-intensity efforts (1-5 reps) or prolonged low-intensity activities.

**The tingling sensation (paresthesia)** that occurs 15-20 minutes after ingestion of doses above 1.6 g is harmless and subsides within 60-90 minutes. It is caused by beta-alanine activating sensory neurons in the skin. Using sustained-release formulations or splitting doses eliminates this effect for most individuals.

### Citrulline Malate

L-citrulline is an amino acid that the kidneys convert to L-arginine, which is then converted to nitric oxide (NO). Citrulline is more effective at raising blood arginine levels than supplemental arginine itself, because citrulline bypasses first-pass metabolism in the liver. Increased NO production causes vasodilation, improving blood flow to working muscles.

**Dosing protocol:** 6-8 g of citrulline malate (2:1 ratio of citrulline to malic acid) consumed 30-60 minutes before training. Some products use L-citrulline without the malate; in that case, 3-5 g of pure L-citrulline is the equivalent dose.

**Expected benefits:** Improved muscular endurance (1-3 additional reps per set at moderate loads), enhanced muscle pump during training, reduced perceived exertion, and faster post-workout recovery (reduced muscle soreness). Wax et al. (2015) found that 8 g of citrulline malate before lower body resistance exercise significantly increased total repetitions performed and reduced muscle soreness 24 and 48 hours post-exercise.

### Omega-3 Fish Oil (EPA and DHA)

Omega-3 fatty acids, specifically eicosapentaenoic acid (EPA) and docosahexaenoic acid (DHA), have anti-inflammatory properties, support cardiovascular health, enhance cell membrane fluidity, and may support muscle protein synthesis.

**Dosing protocol:** 2-3 g combined EPA + DHA per day, taken with meals containing fat. Look for products that list the EPA and DHA content separately (not just "fish oil"); a 1000 mg fish oil capsule may only contain 300-500 mg of combined EPA + DHA. Higher EPA products (2:1 or 3:1 EPA:DHA ratio) may be more effective for anti-inflammatory purposes.

**Expected benefits:** Reduced exercise-induced inflammation and muscle soreness, potential enhancement of MPS when combined with protein intake (Smith et al. 2011 found that 4 g/day of omega-3 increased the MPS response to insulin and amino acid infusion), improved cardiovascular health markers (reduced triglycerides, blood pressure, resting heart rate), and potential cognitive and mood benefits.

### Ashwagandha (Withania somnifera)

Ashwagandha is an adaptogenic herb with a growing body of research supporting its benefits for strength, recovery, and stress management.

**Dosing protocol:** 300-600 mg per day of KSM-66 or Sensoril extract (standardized root extracts with documented potency). Taken daily, preferably with a meal.

**Expected benefits:** Wankhede et al. (2015) found that 300 mg of KSM-66 twice daily for 8 weeks, combined with resistance training, significantly increased bench press 1RM, leg extension 1RM, muscle size, testosterone levels, and reduced exercise-induced muscle damage compared to placebo. Ashwagandha has been shown to reduce cortisol levels by 14-28% (Chandrasekhar et al. 2012), which may support recovery and body composition during periods of high stress. Additional benefits include improved sleep quality, reduced anxiety, and enhanced VO2 max.

## Tier 3: Limited Evidence

### HMB (Beta-Hydroxy Beta-Methylbutyrate)

HMB is a metabolite of the amino acid leucine that has anti-catabolic properties. It may reduce muscle protein breakdown during conditions of high catabolic stress.

**When it may help:** During severe caloric restriction (aggressive cuts), in untrained individuals beginning a resistance training program, and in elderly populations experiencing sarcopenia. For well-trained lifters eating adequate protein, HMB provides minimal additional benefit because leucine from dietary protein already maximizes the same signaling pathways.

**Dosing:** 3 g per day, split into 3 doses of 1 g.

### BCAAs (Branched-Chain Amino Acids)

BCAAs (leucine, isoleucine, valine) were once among the most popular supplements in the fitness industry. However, research has consistently shown that BCAA supplementation is unnecessary when total daily protein intake is adequate (1.6 g/kg or higher). Jackman et al. (2017) found that BCAAs alone stimulated MPS by only 22%, compared to a complete protein source providing the same leucine content which stimulated MPS by 50%. The reason: MPS requires all essential amino acids, not just the branched-chain ones. BCAAs without the other 6 essential amino acids cannot sustain MPS.

**When BCAAs might help:** The only scenario where BCAAs have potential utility is for individuals training in a completely fasted state who cannot consume a complete protein source. Even then, essential amino acids (EAAs) containing all 9 essential amino acids would be a superior choice. For the vast majority of lifters eating adequate protein, BCAAs are an unnecessary expense.

### Glutamine

L-glutamine is the most abundant amino acid in the body and plays roles in immune function, gut barrier integrity, and nitrogen transport. During periods of severe physiological stress (critical illness, major surgery, extensive burns), glutamine requirements exceed the body's production capacity, making supplementation beneficial.

For healthy individuals engaged in resistance training, glutamine supplementation has not been shown to enhance muscle growth, reduce soreness, or improve exercise performance when protein intake is adequate. The gut and immune system consume approximately 70% of supplemental glutamine before it reaches skeletal muscle. Glutamine may have modest benefits for gut health and immune function during periods of intense training combined with caloric restriction, but it is not a priority supplement for body composition.

**Dosing if used:** 5-10 g per day.

### ZMA (Zinc Monomethionine Aspartate, Magnesium Aspartate, Vitamin B6)

ZMA is a combination supplement marketed for its supposed testosterone-boosting and sleep-improving properties. The original study by Brilla and Conte (2000) found that ZMA increased testosterone by 33% and strength by 11% in college football players, but this study has been criticized for methodological issues and the results have not been replicated.

Subsequent research (Koehler et al. 2009) found no significant effects of ZMA on testosterone, strength, or body composition in trained men with adequate zinc and magnesium status. The only scenario where ZMA produces benefits is when the individual is deficient in zinc and/or magnesium, in which case correcting the deficiency (regardless of whether it comes from ZMA or standalone supplements) will restore normal hormonal function. Standalone zinc (15-30 mg/day as zinc picolinate) and magnesium (200-400 mg/day as magnesium glycinate) supplements are typically more cost-effective than the branded ZMA formula.

## No Evidence / Avoid

### Testosterone Boosters

The vast majority of over-the-counter testosterone boosters (tribulus terrestris, D-aspartic acid, fenugreek, tongkat ali, etc.) have little to no evidence supporting meaningful increases in testosterone within the normal physiological range. While some ingredients may show statistically significant increases in testosterone in small studies, the absolute magnitude of the increases is typically insufficient to produce measurable changes in muscle growth or body composition. The ISSN has not issued a position stand supporting any OTC testosterone booster for performance enhancement. Exceptions: Ashwagandha has moderate evidence for modest testosterone increases (see Tier 2), and zinc supplementation corrects testosterone suppression in zinc-deficient individuals.

### Fat Burners

Most commercial fat burners are stimulant-based products containing caffeine, green tea extract, and various thermogenic compounds. The caffeine content is the only ingredient with robust evidence for increasing metabolic rate, and caffeine is far cheaper purchased as a standalone supplement. Other common ingredients (yohimbine, synephrine, capsaicin) have minimal effects. No supplement can produce meaningful fat loss without a caloric deficit. Any supplement claiming to produce fat loss without dietary changes should be viewed with extreme skepticism.

### Detox Products

Products marketed as "detoxes" or "cleanses" have no scientific basis. The liver and kidneys perform detoxification continuously and do not require supplemental assistance in healthy individuals. Detox products typically contain laxatives, diuretics, and/or fiber, which produce temporary weight loss through water and intestinal content loss, not fat loss.

## Timing and Stacking

### Pre-Workout Stack

An evidence-based pre-workout stack taken 30-60 minutes before training consists of caffeine (3-6 mg/kg), citrulline malate (6-8 g), and optionally beta-alanine (if part of the daily loading protocol, timing does not matter for beta-alanine). This combination enhances strength, endurance, focus, blood flow, and fatigue resistance.

### Daily Supplements

Take the following daily with meals: creatine monohydrate (3-5 g, with any meal), omega-3 fish oil (2-3 g EPA+DHA, with a fat-containing meal), vitamin D3 (1000-5000 IU, with a fat-containing meal), and magnesium glycinate (200-400 mg, before bed for sleep quality benefits).

### Post-Workout

A protein shake (25-40 g of whey or plant-based protein) consumed within 2 hours of training supports muscle protein synthesis recovery. This can be combined with a carbohydrate source (banana, dextrose, oats) for glycogen replenishment. Creatine can be taken post-workout if preferred, as some evidence suggests slightly better uptake with the post-workout insulin response, but the difference is negligible.

## ISSN Position Stands Referenced

The International Society of Sports Nutrition has published position stands on several supplements discussed here. The ISSN position stand on protein and exercise (Jager et al. 2017) recommends 1.4-2.0 g/kg/day for active individuals, with higher intakes (2.3-3.1 g/kg of fat-free mass) during caloric restriction. The ISSN position stand on creatine (Kreider et al. 2017) concludes that creatine monohydrate is the most effective ergogenic nutritional supplement available for increasing high-intensity exercise capacity and lean body mass. The ISSN position stand on caffeine (Guest et al. 2021) confirms caffeine's ergogenic effects across aerobic and anaerobic exercise modalities at doses of 3-6 mg/kg. The ISSN position stand on beta-alanine (Trexler et al. 2015) supports its use for improving exercise capacity during 1-4 minute high-intensity efforts. The ISSN position stand on energy drinks and energy shots (Campbell et al. 2013) acknowledges the performance benefits of caffeine-containing energy drinks while cautioning against excessive consumption, particularly in combination with alcohol.

## Supplement Quality and Third-Party Testing

The dietary supplement industry is largely unregulated. Products are not required to prove efficacy before sale, and independent testing has found that many supplements contain less (or more) of the listed ingredients than claimed, or contain contaminants including heavy metals, banned substances, and unlisted pharmaceuticals.

### Third-Party Certifications

To ensure supplement quality and safety, look for products certified by one of the following organizations. NSF Certified for Sport: the most rigorous certification, required by many professional sports organizations and the NFL. Products are tested for label accuracy, contaminant screening (including banned substances), and manufacturing quality. Informed Sport: tests every batch for banned substances and is recognized by WADA (World Anti-Doping Agency). Products bearing this certification are trusted by Olympic and professional athletes. USP Verified: the United States Pharmacopeia tests for purity, potency, and dissolution (whether the product actually breaks down and releases its contents). ConsumerLab: an independent testing service that publishes results for various supplement categories.

### Red Flags in Supplement Marketing

Proprietary blends that list multiple ingredients without individual doses prevent the consumer from knowing whether each ingredient is present in an effective amount. A proprietary blend of 5 g containing creatine, beta-alanine, citrulline, and BCAAs cannot possibly contain effective doses of all four ingredients. Claims of "clinically dosed" should be verified against the actual clinical literature; many products use this term loosely. Before-and-after photos in supplement advertising are not evidence of product efficacy, as the transformations are typically the result of training and diet changes, not the supplement. Unrealistic claims ("gain 10 pounds of muscle in 30 days" or "lose 20 pounds without exercise") are unequivocal signs of a fraudulent product.

### Cost-Effective Supplement Strategy

A minimalist, evidence-based supplement stack that covers the essentials costs approximately $30-50 per month: creatine monohydrate (bulk powder, approximately $0.05 per serving), caffeine (either from coffee or caffeine pills at approximately $0.05 per serving), vitamin D3 (approximately $0.03 per serving), magnesium glycinate (approximately $0.10 per serving), and optionally a protein powder (approximately $0.50-1.00 per serving depending on brand and type). This basic stack addresses the supplements with the strongest evidence while avoiding the expensive, unproven products that consume the majority of the average lifter's supplement budget.

## Supplements for Specific Goals

### For Fat Loss Phase

During a caloric deficit, the following supplements become more valuable. Protein powder becomes increasingly important for hitting elevated protein targets (2.0-2.4 g/kg) when appetite is reduced and whole food intake decreases. Creatine should be maintained throughout a cut to preserve muscle performance and cellular hydration; it does not cause fat gain. Caffeine serves double duty as a performance enhancer and a mild appetite suppressant (though the appetite effect diminishes with chronic use). A multivitamin may provide insurance against micronutrient gaps when food variety and quantity decrease. Omega-3 fish oil supports the anti-inflammatory environment needed when the body is under the metabolic stress of caloric restriction.

### For Muscle Gain Phase

During a caloric surplus, supplementation needs are more basic because the high food intake provides most micronutrients and calories. Creatine remains the top priority for maximizing training capacity. Protein powder is less critical if whole food protein intake is adequate but remains convenient for reaching targets. Citrulline malate before training sessions can support higher training volumes, which is the primary driver of hypertrophy during a surplus phase.

### For General Health and Longevity

Beyond performance, certain supplements support long-term health for active individuals. Vitamin D3 (1000-5000 IU/day) for immune and bone health. Omega-3 fish oil (2-3 g EPA+DHA/day) for cardiovascular health and systemic inflammation reduction. Magnesium (200-400 mg/day) for sleep, muscle function, and metabolic health. Fiber supplements (psyllium husk, 5-10 g/day) if dietary fiber intake falls below 25-30 g/day. A probiotic containing Lactobacillus and Bifidobacterium strains may support gut health, immune function, and nutrient absorption, though the evidence is still developing and strain-specific.

## Common Supplement Myths

**Myth: You need supplements to build muscle.** No supplement is required for muscle growth. Training stimulus, adequate protein (from food), caloric surplus, and sleep are the four pillars. Supplements provide marginal improvements at best.

**Myth: Creatine is a steroid.** Creatine is a naturally occurring compound found in meat and fish, synthesized by the body from amino acids. It has no hormonal effects and no resemblance to anabolic steroids.

**Myth: Protein powder damages the kidneys.** In healthy individuals with normal kidney function, high protein intake (up to 3.0 g/kg/day) has not been shown to cause kidney damage in any controlled study. Individuals with pre-existing kidney disease should consult their physician about protein intake.

**Myth: You need to cycle creatine.** There is no evidence that creatine needs to be cycled (periods of use followed by periods of non-use). Continuous supplementation at 3-5 g/day is safe and effective indefinitely.

**Myth: BCAAs are essential for growth.** If total daily protein intake is adequate (1.6+ g/kg from complete protein sources), BCAAs provide no additional benefit. The money spent on BCAAs is better allocated to quality food or whey protein.

**Myth: Fat burners melt fat while you sleep.** No supplement can produce meaningful fat loss without a caloric deficit. The thermogenic effect of the most effective fat burner ingredients (caffeine, green tea extract) amounts to an additional 50-100 kcal per day at most, which is a rounding error in the context of daily energy balance.
