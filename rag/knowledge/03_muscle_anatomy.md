# Muscle Anatomy and Training Guide

## Chest (Pectoral Muscles)

### Pectoralis Major — Clavicular Head (Upper Chest)

The clavicular head of the pectoralis major originates from the medial half of the clavicle and inserts on the lateral lip of the bicipital groove of the humerus. Its primary functions are shoulder flexion (raising the arm forward and upward), horizontal adduction (bringing the arm across the body), and internal rotation of the humerus. The clavicular head is preferentially activated during incline pressing movements at 30-45 degrees. EMG studies by Trebs et al. (2010) found that a 44-degree incline produced the highest upper pec activation, while angles above 60 degrees shifted emphasis to the anterior deltoid. Optimal exercises include the Incline Dumbbell Press, low-to-high cable flyes, and incline barbell press. Recommended weekly volume is 10-20 sets for the entire chest, with at least one-third dedicated to incline movements for balanced development.

### Pectoralis Major — Sternal Head (Mid and Lower Chest)

The sternal head originates from the sternum, the upper six costal cartilages, and the aponeurosis of the external oblique. It inserts alongside the clavicular head on the humerus. Its primary functions are horizontal adduction, extension of the flexed arm (bringing the arm down from an overhead position), and internal rotation. The sternal head is the larger portion of the pec major and is maximally activated during flat and decline pressing. The Barbell Bench Press on a flat bench is the gold standard for sternal head activation, producing the highest overall pec EMG compared to incline or decline variations. Dumbbell Flyes and cable crossovers from high to low also heavily recruit the sternal head, particularly when the arms cross the midline of the body.

### Pectoralis Minor

The pectoralis minor lies deep to the pectoralis major, originating from ribs 3-5 and inserting on the coracoid process of the scapula. Its primary function is scapular protraction and depression (pulling the shoulder blade forward and down). While not a muscle trained for aesthetics, a tight pectoralis minor contributes to rounded shoulders and shoulder impingement by tilting the scapula anteriorly. Stretching the pec minor through doorway stretches and maintaining scapular retraction strength (via rows and face pulls) prevents this postural dysfunction.

### Common Chest Imbalances

The most common imbalance is overdeveloped sternal (lower) chest relative to the clavicular (upper) chest, resulting from excessive flat bench pressing without adequate incline work. Another common issue is anterior deltoid dominance during pressing, which occurs when the lifter does not retract the scapulae or uses an excessively steep incline. Shoulder impingement from pressing is often caused by excessive internal rotation and insufficient rear deltoid and rotator cuff strength. Balancing pressing volume with pulling volume at a 1:1 ratio prevents this.

## Back

The back is a complex structure comprising multiple muscles that perform pulling, extension, and stabilization functions. Training the back requires both vertical pulling (pulldowns, pull-ups) and horizontal pulling (rows) to develop all components.

### Latissimus Dorsi (Lats)

The latissimus dorsi is the largest muscle of the back, originating from the thoracolumbar fascia (attaching to the spinous processes of T7-L5), the iliac crest, and the lower 3-4 ribs. It inserts on the floor of the bicipital groove of the humerus. Its primary functions are shoulder adduction (pulling the arm toward the body from an abducted position), shoulder extension (pulling the arm backward), and internal rotation. The lats are the primary movers in all vertical pulling movements. EMG research by Lehman et al. (2004) found that wide-grip lat pulldowns produced the highest lat activation compared to narrow or supinated grips, though the differences were modest. Pull-Ups and Lat Pulldowns are the most effective lat exercises. The lats also contribute significantly to rows, particularly when the lifter emphasizes driving the elbows behind the body. Recommended volume: 10-20 direct sets per week across rows and vertical pulls.

### Trapezius (Upper, Middle, Lower Fibers)

The trapezius is a large diamond-shaped muscle covering the upper back and neck. The upper fibers originate from the occipital bone and cervical vertebrae and insert on the lateral clavicle and acromion; they elevate the scapula (shrugging). The middle fibers originate from T1-T5 spinous processes and insert on the acromion and spine of the scapula; they retract the scapula (pulling shoulder blades together). The lower fibers originate from T6-T12 and insert on the spine of the scapula; they depress and upwardly rotate the scapula. The upper traps are trained by shrugs (Dumbbell Shrug, barbell shrug) and are also heavily recruited during deadlifts and Farmer's Walk. The middle traps are targeted by horizontal rows with a focus on squeezing the shoulder blades together, particularly Seated Cable Row and Barbell Row with a pause at the top. The lower traps are trained by prone Y-raises, overhead shrugs, and are activated during pull-ups at the top of the movement.

### Rhomboids

The rhomboids (major and minor) lie deep to the trapezius, originating from the spinous processes of C7-T5 and inserting on the medial border of the scapula. They retract the scapula and assist in downward rotation. They are activated during all rowing movements, particularly when the lifter focuses on squeezing the shoulder blades together at the end of the concentric phase. Weak rhomboids contribute to rounded shoulder posture and anterior shoulder pain.

### Teres Major and Minor

The teres major originates from the inferior angle of the scapula and inserts on the bicipital groove, functioning similarly to the lat (shoulder adduction, extension, internal rotation). It is sometimes called the "little lat" and is trained by the same exercises. The teres minor is part of the rotator cuff (discussed under shoulders), originating from the lateral border of the scapula and inserting on the greater tubercle of the humerus; it externally rotates the shoulder.

### Erector Spinae

The erector spinae group (iliocostalis, longissimus, spinalis) runs along the length of the spine from the sacrum to the skull. These muscles extend the spine (straightening from a bent-over position) and resist flexion during loaded movements. They are isometrically trained during Barbell Rows, deadlifts, squats, and Farmer's Walk, and concentrically trained during back extensions and Good Mornings. Direct erector spinae training with 2-4 sets per week of back extensions or good mornings is sufficient for most lifters, as the erectors receive substantial stimulus from compound movements.

## Shoulders (Deltoids)

### Anterior Deltoid

The anterior deltoid originates from the lateral third of the clavicle and inserts on the deltoid tuberosity of the humerus. Its primary function is shoulder flexion (raising the arm forward) and horizontal adduction. The anterior deltoid is heavily involved in all pressing movements (bench press, overhead press, push-ups) and typically requires little to no direct isolation work for lifters who perform adequate pressing volume. EMG studies show that the Overhead Press and incline press are the most effective anterior delt exercises. Excessive anterior delt development relative to the lateral and posterior heads creates a rounded-shoulder appearance and increases impingement risk.

### Lateral (Middle) Deltoid

The lateral deltoid originates from the acromion of the scapula and inserts on the deltoid tuberosity. Its primary function is shoulder abduction (raising the arm out to the side). The lateral deltoid is responsible for shoulder width and the "capped" shoulder appearance that contributes significantly to the V-taper physique. EMG research consistently shows that the Lateral Raise (dumbbell or cable) produces the highest lateral delt activation. Cable Lateral Raises provide constant tension throughout the range of motion, unlike dumbbells which have minimal tension at the bottom. The lateral deltoid benefits from higher rep ranges (12-20) and higher weekly volumes (12-20 sets) because it is a relatively small muscle that recovers quickly and responds well to metabolic stress.

### Posterior Deltoid

The posterior deltoid originates from the spine of the scapula and inserts on the deltoid tuberosity. Its primary functions are shoulder extension, horizontal abduction (pulling the arm backward in a horizontal plane), and external rotation. The posterior deltoid is the most commonly underdeveloped of the three heads because it is not significantly activated during pressing movements and requires deliberate isolation work. Face Pulls, reverse flyes (cable or dumbbell), and wide-grip rows with an emphasis on pulling toward the face are the most effective exercises. Recommended volume: 12-18 sets per week. Adequate rear delt development is essential for shoulder health and postural balance.

### Rotator Cuff

The rotator cuff consists of four muscles: supraspinatus (initiates abduction), infraspinatus (external rotation), teres minor (external rotation), and subscapularis (internal rotation). These muscles stabilize the glenohumeral joint during all shoulder movements. Weakness or imbalance in the rotator cuff is the primary cause of shoulder impingement and pain in lifters. Preventive exercises include band external rotations, face pulls, and Cuban rotations, performed for 2-3 sets of 15-20 reps as part of the warm-up 2-3 times per week.

## Biceps

### Long Head (Outer Biceps)

The long head of the biceps brachii originates from the supraglenoid tubercle of the scapula (above the glenoid fossa) and inserts on the radial tuberosity via the biceps tendon. Because it crosses the shoulder joint, it is placed in a greater stretch when the arm is extended behind the body (as in an incline curl). EMG studies show that Incline Dumbbell Curls produce the highest long head activation because the shoulder is extended, stretching the long head and increasing its contribution to the curl. The long head contributes to the "peak" appearance of the biceps when viewed from the front.

### Short Head (Inner Biceps)

The short head originates from the coracoid process of the scapula and shares the same insertion as the long head on the radial tuberosity. It is preferentially recruited when the shoulder is flexed or the arm is in front of the body, as in Preacher Curls or spider curls. The short head contributes to biceps width when viewed from the front.

### Brachialis

The brachialis lies deep to the biceps, originating from the anterior surface of the distal humerus and inserting on the coronoid process of the ulna. It is a pure elbow flexor (it does not supinate the forearm) and contributes significantly to arm thickness when viewed from the side. The brachialis is most effectively targeted by Hammer Curls and reverse curls, where the neutral or pronated grip minimizes biceps involvement and forces the brachialis to be the primary mover.

### Brachioradialis

The brachioradialis is technically a forearm muscle but functions as an elbow flexor, particularly with a neutral or pronated grip. It originates from the lateral supracondylar ridge of the humerus and inserts on the styloid process of the radius. Hammer Curls and reverse curls are the most effective exercises. Recommended total biceps volume: 10-16 sets per week, noting that pulling exercises for the back contribute significant indirect biceps stimulus.

## Triceps

### Long Head

The long head of the triceps originates from the infraglenoid tubercle of the scapula (below the glenoid fossa) and inserts on the olecranon process of the ulna. Because it crosses both the shoulder and elbow joints, it is maximally stretched when the shoulder is flexed (arm overhead). Overhead Triceps Extensions (dumbbell, cable, or EZ-bar) are the most effective exercises for the long head due to this stretch position. The long head is the largest of the three heads and contributes the most to overall arm mass.

### Lateral Head

The lateral head originates from the posterior surface of the humerus (above the radial groove) and inserts on the olecranon. It is the most visible head when viewed from the side and is responsible for the horseshoe shape of the triceps. Tricep Pushdowns with a rope or straight bar and close-grip bench press effectively target the lateral head.

### Medial Head

The medial head originates from the posterior surface of the humerus (below the radial groove) and inserts on the olecranon. It lies deep to the lateral and long heads and is active during all elbow extension movements, particularly at lower intensities and during the initial phase of extension. It is trained by any triceps exercise but is most effectively isolated with reverse-grip pushdowns. Recommended total triceps volume: 10-14 sets per week, noting that pressing exercises for chest and shoulders provide significant indirect triceps work.

## Quadriceps

### Rectus Femoris

The rectus femoris is the only quad muscle that crosses both the hip and knee joints, originating from the anterior inferior iliac spine (pelvis) and inserting via the patellar tendon on the tibial tuberosity. It flexes the hip and extends the knee. Because it crosses two joints, it is placed in a greater stretch during exercises where the hip is extended (as in a sissy squat or hack squat with an upright torso). Leg Extensions with a slight posterior pelvic tilt can emphasize the rectus femoris.

### Vastus Lateralis, Medialis, and Intermedius

The three vasti originate from various parts of the femur (not crossing the hip joint) and insert via the patellar tendon. The vastus lateralis (outer quad) is the largest quad muscle and is the primary contributor to the quad sweep visible from the front. The vastus medialis (inner quad, VMO — vastus medialis oblique) is critical for knee stability, particularly during the final degrees of knee extension, and produces the teardrop shape visible above the kneecap. The vastus intermedius lies deep to the rectus femoris and is trained by all knee extension movements.

The Barbell Back Squat is the most effective compound exercise for overall quad development, producing high EMG activation across all four heads. Leg Press provides similar quad stimulus with less spinal loading. Leg Extensions isolate the quads and are particularly effective for the rectus femoris and VMO. Front Squats shift emphasis toward the quads due to the more upright torso position. Bulgarian Split Squats provide unilateral quad training with a significant balance component. Recommended weekly volume: 12-18 sets.

## Hamstrings

### Biceps Femoris (Long Head and Short Head)

The long head of the biceps femoris originates from the ischial tuberosity (sit bone) and inserts on the head of the fibula. It extends the hip and flexes the knee. The short head originates from the linea aspera of the femur and only flexes the knee (it does not cross the hip joint). The biceps femoris forms the lateral hamstring mass.

### Semitendinosus and Semimembranosus

Both originate from the ischial tuberosity and insert on the medial side of the tibia. They extend the hip and flex the knee, forming the medial hamstring mass. The semimembranosus is the broader, deeper muscle, while the semitendinosus has a long, round tendon.

The hamstrings require both hip extension (hip hinge) exercises and knee flexion (leg curl) exercises for complete development because the short head of the biceps femoris is only trained by knee flexion. The Romanian Deadlift is the most effective hip hinge exercise for hamstring development, loading the hamstrings in a stretched position. The Seated Leg Curl produces higher overall hamstring EMG than the lying leg curl because the hip-flexed position pre-stretches the hamstrings. The Nordic Hamstring Curl is the most effective exercise for eccentric hamstring strength and is widely used in injury prevention protocols. Recommended weekly volume: 10-16 sets, roughly balanced between hip extension and knee flexion movements.

## Glutes

### Gluteus Maximus

The gluteus maximus is the largest and most superficial gluteal muscle, originating from the ilium, sacrum, coccyx, and thoracolumbar fascia, and inserting on the iliotibial tract and gluteal tuberosity of the femur. Its primary functions are hip extension (standing up from a squat, climbing stairs), external rotation of the hip, and posterior pelvic tilt. The glute max is maximally activated during exercises that require powerful hip extension against heavy resistance. EMG research by Contreras et al. (2015) found that the Hip Thrust produced the highest glute max activation (mean EMG of 69% MVC) compared to the back squat (50% MVC) and deadlift (55% MVC). However, the squat and deadlift load the glutes through a greater range of motion and under greater absolute loads, making them important complements to hip thrusts. Other effective exercises include the Bulgarian Split Squat, step-ups, and cable pull-throughs. Recommended volume: 10-16 sets per week.

### Gluteus Medius and Minimus

The gluteus medius originates from the outer surface of the ilium and inserts on the greater trochanter. The gluteus minimus lies deep to the medius with a similar origin and insertion. Both muscles abduct the hip (moving the leg away from the midline) and stabilize the pelvis during single-leg stance (walking, running, lunging). Weakness in the glute medius causes hip drop during single-leg activities (Trendelenburg sign) and contributes to knee valgus (inward collapse) during squats. Effective exercises include the Hip Abductor Machine, banded lateral walks, side-lying hip abduction, and single-leg stance work. These muscles benefit from moderate to high reps (12-20) and should be trained 2-3 times per week for individuals with identifiable weakness.

## Calves

### Gastrocnemius

The gastrocnemius is the superficial two-headed calf muscle, with the medial head originating from the medial femoral condyle and the lateral head from the lateral femoral condyle. Both heads insert via the Achilles tendon on the calcaneus (heel bone). The gastrocnemius plantarflexes the ankle (pointing the toes) and assists in knee flexion. Because it crosses the knee joint, it is maximally activated when the knee is straight. Standing Calf Raises and Donkey Calf Raises most effectively target the gastrocnemius. The gastrocnemius has a higher proportion of fast-twitch fibers compared to the soleus, responding better to moderate rep ranges (8-12) with heavier loads.

### Soleus

The soleus lies deep to the gastrocnemius, originating from the posterior surface of the tibia and fibula and inserting via the Achilles tendon. It is a powerful plantarflexor that is predominantly slow-twitch (approximately 80% type I fibers in most individuals), making it resistant to fatigue but requiring higher training volumes and rep ranges for growth. The soleus is most effectively targeted when the knee is bent at approximately 90 degrees, which slackens the gastrocnemius and forces the soleus to be the primary plantarflexor. Seated Calf Raises are the most effective soleus exercise. Recommended calf volume: 12-16 sets per week, divided between standing (gastrocnemius-focused) and seated (soleus-focused) variations.

## Core

### Rectus Abdominis

The rectus abdominis is the "six-pack" muscle, originating from the pubic crest and symphysis and inserting on the xiphoid process and costal cartilages of ribs 5-7. Its primary function is trunk flexion (curling the ribcage toward the pelvis). Contrary to popular belief, you cannot selectively train the "upper" or "lower" abs as the rectus abdominis is a single continuous muscle. However, exercises that move the ribcage toward the pelvis (crunches, Cable Crunch) emphasize the upper portion, while exercises that move the pelvis toward the ribcage (Hanging Leg Raise, reverse crunches) emphasize the lower portion. Hypertrophy of the rectus abdominis is what makes abs visible; however, visibility is primarily determined by body fat percentage (typically below 12-15% for men and 18-22% for women).

### Transverse Abdominis

The transverse abdominis (TVA) is the deepest core muscle, wrapping around the torso like a corset. It originates from the thoracolumbar fascia, iliac crest, inguinal ligament, and inner surfaces of the lower six costal cartilages, and inserts on the linea alba and pubic crest. Its primary function is to compress the abdominal contents and increase intra-abdominal pressure, which stabilizes the spine during heavy lifting. The TVA is trained by bracing exercises (Planks, dead bugs, Pallof Press) and is activated during the Valsalva maneuver used in heavy compound lifts.

### Internal and External Obliques

The external obliques originate from the outer surfaces of ribs 5-12 and insert on the linea alba, pubic tubercle, and iliac crest. They rotate the trunk contralaterally (right external oblique rotates the trunk to the left) and laterally flex the trunk. The internal obliques lie deep to the externals, originating from the thoracolumbar fascia, iliac crest, and inguinal ligament, inserting on ribs 10-12, linea alba, and pubic crest. They rotate the trunk ipsilaterally. The obliques are trained by rotational exercises (Cable Woodchop, Russian Twist, Bicycle Crunch) and anti-rotation exercises (Pallof Press). Recommended total core volume: 6-12 direct sets per week, as the core receives significant indirect training during squats, deadlifts, overhead pressing, and rows.

## Forearms

### Forearm Flexors

The forearm flexor group (flexor carpi radialis, flexor carpi ulnaris, palmaris longus, flexor digitorum superficialis and profundus) occupies the anterior forearm, originating primarily from the medial epicondyle of the humerus. They flex the wrist and fingers and are responsible for grip strength. They are trained indirectly during all pulling movements (deadlifts, rows, pull-ups, curls) and directly by wrist curls and grip-specific training (dead hangs, plate pinches).

### Forearm Extensors

The forearm extensor group (extensor carpi radialis longus and brevis, extensor carpi ulnaris, extensor digitorum) occupies the posterior forearm, originating primarily from the lateral epicondyle. They extend the wrist and fingers and are responsible for the forearm mass visible when the arm is hanging at the side. Reverse Wrist Curls and Reverse Curls target the extensors. Imbalance between flexors and extensors contributes to tennis elbow (lateral epicondylitis) and golfer's elbow (medial epicondylitis).

## Hip Flexors, Adductors, and Abductors

### Hip Flexors (Iliopsoas)

The iliopsoas consists of the psoas major (originating from T12-L5 vertebral bodies) and the iliacus (originating from the iliac fossa), both inserting on the lesser trochanter of the femur. The iliopsoas is the primary hip flexor, raising the thigh toward the trunk. It is heavily recruited during Hanging Leg Raises, sit-ups, and sprinting. Tightness of the hip flexors is extremely common in individuals who sit for prolonged periods and contributes to anterior pelvic tilt and lower back pain. Stretching the hip flexors (kneeling lunge stretch, couch stretch) is important for maintaining posture and squat mechanics.

### Adductors

The adductor group (adductor magnus, longus, brevis, gracilis, pectineus) originates from the pubic bone and ischium and inserts along the medial femur. They adduct the hip (pulling the leg toward the midline) and assist in hip flexion and rotation. The adductors are significantly active during wide-stance squats, sumo deadlifts, and lunges. The Hip Adductor Machine provides direct isolation. Weak adductors contribute to groin strains and knee instability. Recommended: 4-8 sets per week of direct or indirect adductor training.

### Abductors

The primary hip abductors are the gluteus medius, gluteus minimus, and tensor fasciae latae (TFL). They abduct the hip and stabilize the pelvis during single-leg activities. The Hip Abductor Machine, banded lateral walks, and side-lying abduction are the primary isolation exercises. The abductors are trained indirectly during any single-leg exercise (Bulgarian Split Squat, lunges, step-ups). Recommended: 4-8 direct sets per week, especially for individuals with identifiable weakness or knee valgus tendencies.

## Muscle Group Training Priority Guide

### Volume Recommendations Summary

The following table summarizes recommended weekly set ranges per muscle group for hypertrophy in intermediate to advanced lifters, based on Renaissance Periodization guidelines and current literature. These ranges include both direct and indirect volume from compound exercises.

Chest: 10-20 sets per week. Begin at 10, increase by 2 sets per week across a mesocycle. The chest responds well to a variety of angles and exercises. At least one-third of volume should target the upper (clavicular) head through incline movements.

Back (Width — Lats): 10-20 sets per week of vertical and horizontal pulling. The lats are large muscles with high recovery capacity. Alternate between overhand, neutral, and underhand grips to vary the stimulus.

Back (Thickness — Traps, Rhomboids): 10-16 sets per week of rows with an emphasis on scapular retraction. These muscles overlap significantly with lat training, so total back volume can be distributed across both row and pulldown/pull-up variations.

Shoulders (Lateral Deltoid): 12-20 sets per week. The lateral deltoid is a small muscle with high recovery capacity that responds well to high frequency (4-6 sessions per week) and higher rep ranges (12-20 reps). It receives minimal stimulus from pressing movements, so direct isolation is essential.

Shoulders (Rear Deltoid): 12-18 sets per week. The most commonly underdeveloped deltoid head. Face pulls, reverse flyes, and wide-grip rows should be staples.

Shoulders (Anterior Deltoid): 0-8 direct sets per week. The anterior deltoid receives extensive indirect work from all pressing movements (bench press, overhead press, incline press). Most lifters require zero to minimal direct anterior delt work. Overdeveloped anterior delts relative to lateral and posterior heads create an unbalanced, rounded-shoulder appearance.

Biceps: 10-16 sets per week. Many of these sets come from pulling exercises. Direct bicep work (curls) of 6-10 sets per week is typically sufficient when combined with back training.

Triceps: 10-14 sets per week. Many of these sets come from pressing exercises. Direct tricep work (extensions, pushdowns) of 6-10 sets per week is typically sufficient when combined with chest and shoulder pressing.

Quadriceps: 12-18 sets per week. Squats, leg press, lunges, and leg extensions all contribute. The quads are large, strong muscles that require substantial volume but also generate significant systemic fatigue through compound movements.

Hamstrings: 10-16 sets per week. Approximately half should come from hip hinge movements (Romanian deadlift, good mornings) and half from knee flexion exercises (leg curls). This ensures both the long head of the biceps femoris (which crosses two joints) and the short head (which only crosses the knee) are adequately stimulated.

Glutes: 10-16 sets per week. Squats, deadlifts, hip thrusts, and lunges all contribute. Individuals who want to maximize glute development should include at least one hip extension exercise (hip thrust, glute bridge) and one squat pattern exercise per session.

Calves: 12-16 sets per week divided between standing calf raises (targeting the gastrocnemius with straight knees) and seated calf raises (targeting the soleus with bent knees). The calves are endurance-oriented muscles that recover quickly and benefit from high training frequency (3-6 sessions per week) and a full range of motion with 2-3 second pauses at both the stretched and contracted positions.

Core: 6-12 direct sets per week. The core receives substantial indirect training during squats, deadlifts, overhead pressing, and rows. Direct work should include anti-extension exercises (planks, ab wheel rollouts), spinal flexion exercises (cable crunches, hanging leg raises), and anti-rotation exercises (Pallof press, cable woodchops).

Forearms: 4-8 direct sets per week, if training them directly at all. Most lifters receive sufficient forearm stimulus from pulling exercises (deadlifts, rows, pull-ups, curls). Direct forearm training is recommended for individuals with grip-limited deadlifts, those interested in arm aesthetics, or those with tendinopathy who need targeted rehabilitation work.

## Common Muscle Imbalances and Corrections

### Anterior-Posterior Imbalance (Front vs. Back)

The most common imbalance in recreational lifters is overdeveloped anterior muscles (chest, anterior deltoids, biceps) relative to posterior muscles (back, rear deltoids, triceps long head). This results from the tendency to prioritize "mirror muscles" and bench pressing while neglecting rows, rear delt work, and scapular stability. Consequences include rounded shoulders, forward head posture, shoulder impingement risk, and upper back pain. Correction: implement a 1.5:1 pull-to-push ratio for 3-6 months (e.g., if performing 15 sets of pushing per week, perform 22-23 sets of pulling). Emphasize face pulls, rear delt flyes, and rows with a scapular retraction hold.

### Quad-Hamstring Imbalance

Overdeveloped quadriceps relative to hamstrings is common in lifters who squat frequently but neglect hip hinge movements and leg curls. The hamstrings should produce approximately 60-80% of the force that the quadriceps can produce (hamstring-to-quadriceps ratio). A ratio below 50% increases the risk of hamstring strains and ACL injuries. Correction: include at least one hip hinge exercise (Romanian deadlift, good morning) and one knee flexion exercise (seated or lying leg curl) in every lower body session. Nordic hamstring curls are particularly effective for building eccentric hamstring strength.

### Glute Medius Weakness

Weak gluteus medius muscles cause the hip to drop on the unsupported side during single-leg activities (Trendelenburg sign), contribute to knee valgus (inward collapse) during squats and lunges, and are associated with IT band syndrome, patellofemoral pain, and ankle instability. This weakness is extremely common due to sedentary lifestyles. Correction: include banded lateral walks, side-lying hip abduction, single-leg stance work, and hip abductor machine exercises 2-3 times per week.

### Upper-Lower Trap Imbalance

Overdeveloped upper traps (from shrugging, poor deadlift form, and stress-related tension) relative to the lower traps (which depress and upwardly rotate the scapula) contributes to elevated shoulders, neck tension, and impaired overhead mobility. Correction: reduce shrugging volume, add prone Y-raises and overhead shrugs, and focus on scapular depression during pull-ups and pulldowns.
